# **Character Overview**

*She wanted to stop being invisible. She wanted them to notice her. The hag gave her exactly what she asked for \- and now she can never be unseen again.*

**Birth Name:** Echo (no surname until the circus)

**Stage Name:** Echo 'The Spider Lady' Nyorsk

**Race:** Wood Elf (transformed into Silkborn)

**Class:** Cleric, Shadow Domain

**Age:** 56 years old

**Current Location:** Icehaven (arrived days ago)

**Current State:** Emotionally closed off, barely any coin, just wants to be left alone

## **The Curse**

Echo's transformation granted her the Silkborn form \- multiple red eyes hidden beneath her hair, mandible markings at the corners of her mouth, pointed ears retained from her elven heritage, and the ability to produce silk from her own body. She hides her features with veils, fans, and careful styling, appearing as a beautiful if mysterious elven woman.

But her true curse lies in reflection. Whenever Echo looks into a mirror, still water, polished metal, or any reflective surface \- she sees her true self: a shadowy spider-humanoid with no features. Anyone looking at the same reflection sees it too. This curse has destroyed every chance at normalcy, every relationship, every moment of peace.

# **Part I: The Nobody**

*Age 0-50 | Uthodurn*

*In the underground city where elves and dwarves carved civilization from stone, two wood elf children survived on the streets \- forgotten, hungry, invisible. Echo was the one who kept them alive.*

### **The Urchin Years**

Echo never knew her parents. Her earliest memories are of cold stone floors in Uthodurn's lower warrens, of scrounging for scraps, of learning which merchants would chase you and which would look away. She was not alone in this \- beside her was Kimiko, another wood elf orphan, and in those desperate years they were everything to each other.

But even then, the dynamic was clear. Echo was the provider, the protector, the one who found food and shelter and safety. She was the leader. Kimiko followed, grateful and dependent, and Echo never minded. They were sisters in all but blood.

### **The Weaving Master**

Their lives changed when Manuel Tafri found them \- a dwarven weaving master with a keen eye and a soft heart. He took them both in, gave them beds and meals and purpose. Under his tutelage, both girls learned the craft of weaving, and both showed talent.

But not equal talent.

Echo's work was exceptional. Her patterns were intricate, her technique flawless, her artistic vision remarkable for someone so young. She poured her soul into every thread, creating pieces that should have made her famous.

They didn't.

### **The Invisible Girl**

Echo was socially awkward in ways she couldn't understand or fix. She said the wrong things at the wrong times. She missed cues that seemed obvious to everyone else. She couldn't make people comfortable, couldn't charm them, couldn't make them want to give her a chance.

Kimiko could.

Kimiko's work was good \- competent, even skilled \- but it wasn't exceptional. It didn't matter. She knew how to smile at the right moments, how to laugh at jokes, how to make patrons feel special. Her charm opened doors that Echo's talent couldn't.

Slowly, over years, the dynamic shifted. The girl Echo had protected became the star. The girl who had depended on Echo began to forget what Echo had done for her. Fame and success proved more intoxicating than old loyalty.

There was no dramatic betrayal \- just a gradual souring, a friendship that faded as Kimiko grew addicted to attention and pushed her true friend to the sideline. After fifty years of this, Echo was exactly where she'd started: invisible, overlooked, alone.

The girl who once kept them both alive was now the one being forgotten.

# **Part II: The Bargain**

*Age \~51 | Uthodurn*

*The Ironbloom Festival should have been her moment. Instead, it became the night everything changed.*

### **The Ironbloom Festival**

Once a year, Uthodurn celebrates the Ironbloom Festival \- a grand event where the finest crafts are displayed for nobles, VIPs, and wealthy visitors from across Wildemount. For weavers, it is the ultimate showcase. Careers are made and broken in those few days.

Echo had prepared for months. Her finest work, a tapestry that captured the bioluminescent beauty of Uthodurn's fungal gardens in thread and light. She had poured everything into it \- her skill, her vision, her desperate hope that this time, someone would see her.

They didn't.

A visiting noble examined her work with genuine admiration \- and then Kimiko appeared, all smiles and charm, and within minutes the noble was commissioning work from her instead. Not because Kimiko's samples were better. Simply because Kimiko was easier to like.

Echo watched it happen. Again. As always.

But this time was worse. In her desperation to make an impression, she spoke too eagerly, said something awkward, something wrong. The noble's expression shifted from interest to discomfort to dismissal. Nearby merchants noticed. Whispers spread. By the end of the evening, Echo had become a joke \- the strange girl who couldn't read a room, who drove away customers with her desperation.

She fled into the alleys, tears streaming down her face, wanting only to disappear.

### **Auntie Zeli**

The house shouldn't have been there.

Echo knew these alleys. She had survived in them as a child. But in her blind, grief-stricken wandering, she found a door she'd never seen before, leading to a small dwelling that seemed to exist outside the city's logic.

Inside was an old woman. Kind eyes. Warm smile. A cup of tea already steaming.

"Come in, dear," she said. "You look like you need someone to talk to."

Her name was Auntie Zeli, and she was the first person in Echo's life who truly seemed to understand. She listened without judgment. She acknowledged Echo's pain without minimizing it. She saw Echo \- really saw her \- in a way no one else ever had.

For a few weeks, Echo returned to that impossible house again and again. Auntie Zeli became mentor, confidante, the mother figure she'd never had. The old woman taught her things about presentation, about confidence, about believing in herself.

And then, one evening, she made an offer.

### **The Deal**

"I can help you, dear," Auntie Zeli said, her kind eyes gleaming in the candlelight. "I can give you what you truly want. You only have to ask."

Echo knew, somewhere deep down, that this was wrong. That offers like this came with prices. But fifty years of invisibility, of watching lesser work succeed while hers was ignored, of being forgotten by the girl she'd once protected \- it had worn her down to nothing.

"I want to stop being invisible," Echo whispered. "I want them to notice me."

Auntie Zeli smiled.

The transformation was immediate. Pain ripped through Echo's body as her form twisted, as new eyes opened on her forehead, as her mouth changed, as something fundamentally wrong rewrote her very being. She screamed, collapsed, and when she finally looked up, Auntie Zeli was gone.

In her place stood a hag \- ancient, terrible, satisfied.

"The deal is made," the creature said, her voice no longer warm. "No need for charades anymore." She seemed pleased, genuinely pleased, as she faded into shadow. "You wanted to be noticed, dear. Now you will be. Forever."

The house dissolved around Echo, leaving her alone in an empty alley, her new form reflected in a puddle at her feet.

She saw what she had become: a shadowy spider-thing with no features, no identity, no humanity.

She would never stop being noticed again.

# **Part III: The Rise and Fall**

*Age 51-52 | Uthodurn & Surrounding Villages*

*For a few precious months, Echo had everything she ever wanted. Then Kimiko took it away one final time.*

### **The New Echo**

The curse came with gifts. Echo discovered she could produce silk from her own body \- material of extraordinary quality, unlike anything she'd ever worked with. She also discovered that she was now magnetic, alluring, possessed of a presence that drew eyes and held attention.

She learned to hide her monstrous features. A veil over her mouth to conceal the mandible markings. Her hair styled carefully to cover the extra eyes on her forehead. She learned never, ever to look at reflective surfaces.

And then she began to sell her work.

It was like magic \- though of course, it was exactly that. Patrons who would have ignored her before now sought her out. Her charm, her presence, her inexplicable allure made people want to buy what she offered. Combined with her genuine skill and the extraordinary quality of her new silk, Echo finally became what she'd always deserved to be: successful.

She sold in Uthodurn. She traveled to surface villages and sold there too. Her reputation grew. For the first time in her life, Echo was almost famous.

### **The Exposure**

Kimiko noticed.

The girl who had pushed Echo aside couldn't ignore her sudden rise. Curiosity turned to suspicion. How had the awkward, invisible weaver suddenly become so magnetic? Where was this extraordinary silk coming from?

Kimiko followed her. Watched her. And one day, hidden in the shadows of Echo's workshop, she witnessed the truth: Echo producing silk from her own body, her monstrous fingers working the webbing into thread.

Kimiko could have kept the secret. Could have confronted Echo privately. Could have shown mercy to the girl who had once kept her alive on the streets.

She didn't.

The exposure was public and dramatic. At a market gathering in Uthodurn, before a crowd of merchants and patrons, Kimiko ripped the veil from Echo's face. She told everyone what she had seen \- the webbing, the transformation, the monster hiding in plain sight.

"She has been making you wear her body\!" Kimiko cried. "Every piece of silk \- it comes from her\! From this... this thing\!"

The crowd recoiled. People who had admired Echo moments before now stared in horror. The words came fast and cruel: monster, abomination, creature. Someone threw something. Others screamed.

One voice rose in Echo's defense \- a young dwarven merchant named Torvin Ashmantle, who had purchased Echo's work and found her strange but kind. He tried to calm the crowd, tried to argue that her craft was still beautiful regardless of its source.

No one listened.

Echo ran. She didn't confront Kimiko, didn't try to explain, didn't even look back. She just ran, the shouts of the crowd echoing behind her, her brief taste of success turned to ash in her mouth.

Behind her, Kimiko watched her go \- and felt vindicated. Finally, proof that she was the real talent. Finally, proof that Echo had always been wrong, always been less, always been something that didn't belong.

She never considered that she owed Echo her life.

# **Part IV: The Wandering**

*Age 52-53 | Southward Through Wildemount*

*She learned to use her curse as a tool. She also learned that some wounds don't heal.*

### **The Road South**

Exiled and alone, Echo fled south. She had no destination, no plan, only the desperate need to be anywhere else. Her charm \- the hag's gift \- still worked, and she learned to use it for survival. She manipulated caravan drivers into giving her passage. She talked her way into meals and shelter.

But she kept to herself. She hid her face. She learned quickly that her disguise wasn't perfect \- sometimes the veil slipped, sometimes someone looked too closely. The reactions were always the same: horror, disgust, flight. Each time, she moved on. Each time, she built her walls a little higher.

### **The Lake**

Somewhere on the road, she met a man.

He was kind. Curious about the mysterious veiled woman traveling alone. Echo stitched his wounds for him with her new found magic. He asked questions, and for once, Echo found herself answering \- carefully, cautiously, but honestly. He didn't push about the veil. He accepted her boundaries.

They spent an evening by a lake, sitting on the shore, talking for hours as the stars emerged. Echo was happy \- genuinely happy, for the first time since the transformation. She let herself imagine a different future. A connection. Maybe even love.

He asked if he could kiss her. She told him to close his eyes.

He did. And then he opened them.

The lake was still as glass. In its surface, he saw what sat beside him \- not a beautiful elven woman, but a shadowy spider-thing, a creature of nightmare, a monster without features or humanity.

He looked at her face. Saw the mandibles. Saw the eyes she'd tried to hide. Understood.

He ran.

Echo sat alone by the lake until dawn, watching her monstrous reflection ripple in the water. She didn't cry \- she couldn't, not properly, not anymore. Something had broken that could never be fixed.

She gave up on love that night.

### **Shadycreek Run**

Eventually, she reached Shadycreek Run \- the lawless city in the Greying Wildlands where questions weren't asked and monsters weren't the strangest thing on the streets. She survived there, selling her work when she could, hiding when she couldn't.

And there, she met them: Grax the Gentle, a bugbear with kind eyes and an enormous heart, and the Gemini Twins \- Castor and Pollux, changeling siblings who knew what it meant to hide your true form.

They were members of Cornelius's Fantastical Cavalcade of Wonders, and they looked at Echo without horror.

"You are beautiful," Grax said, his voice impossibly gentle for such a fearsome creature. "Just the way you are."

"You would be welcomed among us," the Twins said, their forms shifting as they spoke. "We are all strange. We are all wonderful. You would fit right in."

Echo was surprised. Suspicious. But also hopeful \- desperately, dangerously hopeful.

She didn't believe them. Not immediately. But when she arrived at the circus and saw so many weird, wonderful, impossible people \- all of them celebrated rather than feared \- something cracked in the walls she'd built around herself.

For the first time in years, she felt like she might belong.

# **Part V: The Circus**

*Age 53-55 | Cornelius's Fantastical Cavalcade of Wonders*

*They gave her a name. They gave her a family. Then the world took it all away.*

### **Echo the Spider Lady**

The circus gave her everything. A stage name \- "Echo the Spider Lady Nyorsk" \- in the old carnival tongue that meant flickering, blinking, the moment between moments. A role \- not as a freak to be gawked at, but as a performer, creating silk ribbons during a beautiful dance that left audiences gasping in wonder.

For two years, Echo lived among the strange and the marvelous. She formed connections \- tentative at first, then deeper. Whisper, the kenku mime, became her closest friend \- perhaps because their communication was never about words, and Echo had always struggled with words. They understood each other in silence.

She never told anyone her full story. The hag, Kimiko, Uthodurn \- she left all of it behind. But sometimes, late at night, someone would find her sitting alone, and they would say: "Whenever you're ready to face your past, we'll be there for you."

She was starting to believe them. Starting to feel like this might be home. Starting to let herself imagine staying forever.

### **The Raid**

The attack came without warning.

Hooded figures \- over two dozen of them \- descended on the Cavalcade with military precision. They weren't there to kill everyone. They were there to take. Echo saw circus members fall \- people she'd laughed with, performed with, started to love. She saw Grax fighting desperately before being overwhelmed. She saw the Gemini Twins dragged toward waiting wagons.

She saw them take Whisper.

And then they took her.

Her captor was human \- tall, scarred, with cold eyes and rough hands. He bound her like cargo and threw her into a wagon with the others. As they rode away from the burning circus, Echo heard fragments of conversation between the captors.

"The Shepherd is waiting," one of them said.

She didn't know what it meant. She didn't care. She only knew that the family she'd just started to trust was being torn apart, and she was powerless to stop it.

### **The Escape**

For several days, Echo played the role she'd learned on the road \- the helpless captive, the frightened victim, the beautiful monster who needed protection. Her captor began to relax around her. Began to see her as a prize rather than a threat.

She used her charm. Her presence. The curse that had ruined everything else in her life finally served a purpose.

When the moment came, she acted. A whispered word of Command \- "Run" \- and her captor's body obeyed before his mind could resist. As he fled into the darkness, Echo climbed the wall of the building where they'd stopped, her spider-climb carrying her up and through a window.

She ran. For what felt like days, she ran, not knowing where the others were, not knowing if anyone else had escaped, not knowing anything except that she had to survive.

She didn't know what happened to the other captives. She still doesn't. But she heard them splitting up, each group carrying different prisoners in different directions. The Shepherd \- whoever or whatever that was \- had plans for all of them.

The moment she had started to feel like she belonged to a family, it had been destroyed.

She wouldn't make that mistake again.

# **Part VI: The Flight to Icehaven**

*Age 55-56 | Northward Through Wildemount*

*In winter, everyone covers their face against the cold. Perfect for hiding. Perfect for disappearing. Perfect for giving up.*

### **The Long Road**

After the escape, Echo traveled north. Not because she had a destination \- because north meant winter, and winter meant people wrapped themselves in scarves and hoods and asked no questions about why a woman kept her face covered.

She'd heard of Icehaven. A port town at the edge of civilization. People coming and going constantly \- sailors, traders, travelers, refugees. A place where being strange wasn't strange at all. A place where she might finally blend in.

The journey took almost a year. She survived the way she always had \- charm, manipulation, selling her work when she could, moving on before anyone looked too closely. The usual hardships of the road.

She was running from everything. The kidnappers who might still be hunting escaped cargo. The hag who might have plans she didn't understand. Kimiko, who had destroyed her once and might do so again. The memories of the circus, of belonging, of hope.

Mostly, she was running from the realization that every time she let herself care about something, it was taken away.

### **The Decision**

Somewhere on the road north, Echo made a choice. Not a dramatic moment of revelation \- just a quiet settling of resolve, a wall being built so high that nothing could breach it.

She would not get close to anyone again. She would not let herself hope for family, for love, for belonging. The lake had taught her that romance was impossible. The circus had taught her that community was temporary. Her entire life had taught her that caring led to pain.

From now on, she would focus on survival. On coin. On being left alone.

It wasn't a good life. But it was the only one she could bear.

# **Part VII: Icehaven**

*Age 56 | Present Day*

*She arrived in the frozen port with barely any coin and no intention of staying long enough to call it home. But even the best-laid plans have a way of unraveling.*

### **The Current State**

Echo has been in Icehaven for only a few days. She's staying at an inn \- she doesn't care which one, they're all the same \- and her funds are running dangerously low. She hasn't started selling her work yet; she's still getting settled, still learning the rhythms of the town, still deciding if it's safe to stay.

Mentally, she's closed off. Exhausted. Done. Fifty-six years old by elven standards is barely adulthood, but Echo feels ancient. She's been an orphan, a protector, a failure, a monster, a performer, a captive, a survivor. Each identity stripped away until nothing remains but the need to exist.

She doesn't want friends. Doesn't want connections. Doesn't want anything except to be left alone with enough coin to stop worrying about tomorrow.

The irony isn't lost on her. She spent fifty years wanting to be noticed. Now all she wants is to disappear.

### **Part One: The Covenant of the Sundered Brand**

The Covenant of the Sundered Brand is an ancient order dating back to the tail end of the Calamity, when the Betrayer Gods were banished behind the Divine Gate. In the chaos of those final days, not all vestiges of the Betrayer Gods were destroyed \- some were too dangerous to unmake, and others were necessary to contain something worse.

The Covenant was founded by seven mortal families who witnessed what they call The Branding \- a moment when a dying champion of Bane, the Strife Emperor, was struck down but could not be killed. His essence, tied to a fragment of Bane's divine war-fury, condensed into a relic: The Ember of Dominion.

The Prime Deities, through intermediaries, charged these families with a sacred duty: the Ember could not be destroyed, for its destruction might create a beacon that draws Bane's attention from beyond the Gate. It must be guarded, contained, and forgotten by the world.

The seven families scattered across Exandria. Over centuries, some lines died out, others were compromised. By the modern age, only three bloodlines remain, and only one actively guards the Ember itself.

### **Part Two: House Tigalio of Port Damali**

#### ***The Cover***

Port Damali offers the perfect disguise: a bustling trade city where wealthy merchant families are common, questions are few, and the Clovis Concord's politics provide excellent noise to hide in. The city's cosmopolitan nature also means a hobgoblin fleeing through the region isn't immediately killed on sight \- the Menagerie Coast is more tolerant than the Empire.

The Tigalio family presents themselves as successful wine merchants with vineyards in the hills northeast of the city. "Tigalio Red" is a reasonably well-regarded vintage \- not prestigious enough to draw scrutiny, successful enough to justify their comfortable estate. They are known as a large, close-knit, somewhat private family with old-fashioned values and an unusual devotion to Erathis, the Law Bearer.

This devotion to Erathis is itself a misdirection. The family's true faith is in the duty itself \- they serve no god openly, for their task predates the current order of things. Some family members genuinely worship Erathis; others worship Pelor or the Wildmother. But all of them know: the family's true purpose comes first.

#### ***The Family Tree***

**The Patriarch (Deceased):** Gavros Vorensi Tigalio I \- Kaelen's grandfather, died fifteen years ago. A granite-faced man who spoke rarely and meant every word. He chose to be interred in the family crypt \- directly beside the vault holding the Ember, so that even in death, a Tigalio would stand guard. The children called him "the Silence" behind his back, half in fear, half in reverence.

### **Part Three: The Early Years**

#### ***The Weight of a Name***

Kaelen was seven years old when he first understood that his family was different.

It happened during the Festival of the Dawning, when all of Port Damali spilled into the streets to celebrate the longest day of the year. Matthon had convinced him to sneak away from Orenna's watchful eye, promising there would be honey cakes and fire-dancers near the harbor.

"Come on, Kael," Matthon had said, using the nickname that made him feel grown-up. "Father won't even notice. He's doing the boring wine thing with the Concord officials."

They made it three streets before Sareena found them. She appeared from an alleyway like a ghost, nineteen years old and already terrifying, her dark hair pulled back in the severe style she would keep for the rest of her life.

"Going somewhere?" Her voice was calm, which was somehow worse than if she'd shouted.

Something flickered across Sareena's face \- not anger, but something sadder. She knelt down, bringing herself to their level, and for a moment she looked less like their stern older sister and more like someone carrying a weight too heavy for her years.

*"Someday, you'll understand why we can't just be like everyone else. Why we have to be careful. Why we have to be ready. Especially you, little brother. You have the look of Grandfather about you."*

#### ***The First Lesson***

Sareena began training Kaelen when he was nine. Not Matthon \- Matthon had already shown his talents lay elsewhere, in the ledgers and the charm offensive required to move wine across the Menagerie Coast. But Kaelen had what Sareena called "the instinct."

Their first real lesson took place in the eastern courtyard, away from the household staff's prying eyes. Sareena handed him a wooden practice sword that felt absurdly heavy in his small hands.

"Hit me," she said.

He tried. She sidestepped without seeming to move, and he stumbled past her, nearly falling. For an hour, until his arms burned and tears of frustration pricked at his eyes. Not once did he come close to touching her.

*"You need to know how it feels to face something you can't beat. Someday, you might face something worse than me. Something that won't stop when you're tired, won't give you time to catch your breath. When that day comes, you don't win by being stronger. You win by being ready. By knowing that giving up isn't an option."*

#### ***The Secret in the Cellar***

The day Kaelen learned the truth was his twelfth birthday.

His father came to his room after the small family celebration and told him to dress warmly. "There's something you need to see."

They descended into the wine cellars, past the rows of barrels, past the locked storage rooms, to a wall that looked no different from any other. Vorensi pressed his hand to a particular stone, and Kaelen felt more than heard something shift in the wall itself.

They walked down a stairway that had no right to exist, into a chamber carved from the bedrock beneath Port Damali. And there, behind a door covered in symbols that seemed to shift when Kaelen looked directly at them, his father stopped.

"I won't show you what's inside. Not yet. You're not ready, and truthfully, I hope you never have to see it. But you need to know it's here. You need to know what we protect."

And then his father told him \- about the Calamity, about the Ember, about the seven families reduced to three, about the duty that had defined the Tigalios for centuries before Port Damali even existed.

When it was over, Kaelen stood in silence for a long moment. Then he asked the question that would define him:

*"What happens if we fail?"*

His father's face was unreadable. "We don't."

### **Part Four: The Man Before**

Kaelen grew into the man his family expected \- perhaps even hoped for. By twenty-six, he had completed his paladin oaths, binding himself not to a god but to an ideal of protection. Not of a nation, not of a faith, but of a duty older than nations and faiths. He prayed to Erathis because she represented order, and order was what kept dangerous things contained.

He was kind, if formal. He had friends in the city \- fellow young nobles, a few merchants' children. There was Dael, the sparring partner with the quick smile and quicker blade, who made Kaelen feel something he tried not to examine too closely. He was known as a man who kept his word, paid his debts, and smiled too rarely.

He trained with Sareena three times a week. He studied the old texts with Lysara. He shared quiet dinners with his parents and tried not to notice how his father's hair had gone fully grey, how his mother's hands shook slightly when she thought no one was watching.

He took his turns guarding the vault. Forty times, perhaps more. Always quiet. Always uneventful.

He was, by all accounts, exactly what a Tigalio should be.

The forty-first night was different.

### **Part Five: The Night Everything Ended**

The vault beneath the family crypt is accessible through a hidden passage in the wine cellars. The rotation meant that one family member would spend each night in the antechamber \- not touching the Ember, simply watching. Ensuring the wards held. Being present.

On this night, Kaelen heard a sound that didn't belong.

Three figures. Cloaked in shadow that moved like liquid. Their faces were not obscured \- they simply weren't there. Where features should be, there was only darkness that swallowed light.

They moved past his position as if he wasn't there. He later realized they hadn't expected a guard \- the wards should have been enough. His presence was a variable they hadn't accounted for.

He shouted. Drew his blade. One of them turned, and he felt a pressure in his mind \- not words, but a command to be still. He pushed through it. A Tigalio does not yield.

The fight was brief and brutal. They were not warriors, but they had magic that twisted space, that made his sword pass through them like smoke. He couldn't stop them. They reached the inner vault. The door \- sealed with blood-wards keyed to his family line \- they opened it with a vial of something dark and wrong.

He saw the Ember for the first time.

It was smaller than he'd imagined. A fist-sized crystal of deep crimson, shot through with veins of black that pulsed like a heartbeat. It sat on a plinth of ancient stone, surrounded by runes that had faded with centuries.

One of the shadow-faced figures reached for it.

Kaelen didn't think. He couldn't let them take it. His whole life had been building to this moment, this duty. He threw himself forward, grabbed the figure's arm \-

And his hand closed around the Ember instead.

#### ***The Transformation***

The pain was not physical \- or not only physical. It was the sensation of something else forcing its way into spaces inside him he didn't know existed.

His sword clattered from fingers that were stretching, thickening, turning a mottled grey-green. His armor groaned as his frame expanded. He felt his face reshaping \- bones shifting beneath skin that was no longer his own. He screamed, and the sound that came out was deeper, rougher, wrong.

The shadow-faced figures fled. Whether in fear or because their mission had somehow been completed, he doesn't know. He barely noticed. He was falling to his knees, clutching at his own face, feeling tusks where his teeth had been.

The face that looked back in the mirror was not his.

Grey-green skin pulled tight over a broader skull. Small tusks jutting from a heavier jaw. Yellow eyes with slitted pupils. The features of a hobgoblin warrior, the kind he'd been trained to fight.

But the expression \- the horror in those yellow eyes \- that was still Kaelen.

#### ***The Confrontation***

He was still staring at his reflection when he heard footsteps. Running. And a voice he knew better than his own.

"Kaelen? KAELEN\!"

Sareena burst into the vault chamber, sword drawn, face pale with fear. She had heard the commotion from above. She had come to help.

She saw: The inner vault, door hanging open. The plinth, empty. The shattered wards. Her brother's sword on the ground, covered in blood. His tabard, torn and bloody, crumpled near the entrance. His blood \- she knew his blood, she had sparred with him for fifteen years, she knew what his blood looked like when he was cut \- everywhere.

And standing in the middle of it all, reflected in that ancient mirror, a hobgoblin.

A hobgoblin with her brother's blood on its hands.

For one frozen moment, their eyes met.

"Sareena \-"

His voice. But not his voice. Deeper. Rougher. The voice of a monster speaking her name with her brother's inflection.

*"What did you do to him. WHAT DID YOU DO TO MY BROTHER?"*

She came at him with everything she had.

Kaelen had trained under her for fifteen years. He knew her patterns, her tells, the way she favored her left side when angry. But his body was different now \- longer reach, different balance, unfamiliar weight. He couldn't fight her. He could barely defend.

Her blade caught his arm. His blood \- darker now, not quite red \- spattered the stone.

He ran.

He ran because he couldn't fight her. He ran because even if he could, he wouldn't. He ran because the alternative was dying at his sister's hands, and she would have to live with that forever, even if she never knew the truth.

### **Part Six: The Last Glimpse**

He didn't go far. He couldn't.

The forest northeast of the estate \- he knew every trail, every hiding spot from childhood games of chase with Matthon and Lysara. He found a hollow beneath an old oak, pressed himself into the darkness, and waited.

When the moon rose, he crept to the edge of the treeline. From there, he could see the manor. The windows glowed warm with lamplight \- too much lamplight for this hour. The whole household was awake.

He watched.

He saw his father standing rigid in the main hall, hands clasped behind his back, the way he stood when he was trying not to fall apart. Saw his mother \-

Cressida Tigalio was not a woman who cried. She had buried her father-in-law with dry eyes and steady hands. She had faced threats to the family with cold pragmatism.

She was on her knees in the hall. Sobbing. Her whole body shaking with it.

Sareena stood before her parents, Kaelen's sword in her hands. The blade was still dark with blood \- his blood, his old blood, from before. She was speaking. He couldn't hear the words through the glass, but he didn't need to.

She was telling them he was dead.

He watched his mother break.

He watched his father put a hand on his wife's shoulder, face carved from stone, and he knew that expression \- knew it meant his father was screaming inside where no one could hear.

He watched Matthon slump into a chair, head in his hands. Watched Lysara standing apart, arms wrapped around herself, face pale and lost.

He watched his family mourn him.

And he understood, with a clarity that felt like another kind of transformation, what he had to do.

He couldn't go to them. Not like this. Sareena's reaction had proven that \- even if he could make them listen, even if they believed him, the Covenant's laws were clear. The Ember must be recovered or destroyed. If it couldn't be separated from its host, the host must be destroyed.

His family would have to kill him. Or try to. And either way, it would destroy them worse than his death already had.

No.

He had to find another way. He had to find someone who could reverse this \- separate the Ember from his body, restore him to what he was. And until then, he had to stay far, far away from everyone he loved.

For their sake.

For his own.

Kaelen Vorensi Tigalio III turned away from the only home he had ever known, and walked into the darkness.

### **Part Seven: Sareena's Vendetta**

In the days that followed, Sareena barely slept.

She coordinated with the Concord guard to put out descriptions of the hobgoblin. She interrogated every contact the family had. She sent word to the other bloodlines, warning them that the Ember had been taken, that a creature with unknown abilities was loose in the world.

But she didn't trust anyone else to lead the hunt.

The creature had spoken her name. In a voice that wasn't Kaelen's but knew things Kaelen knew. It had moved with a strange hesitation, like it was unfamiliar with its own body. It had looked at her with something that might have been recognition.

Sareena had a theory. She didn't share it, because saying it aloud would make it real.

She believed the hobgoblin hadn't just killed her brother. She believed it had consumed him somehow. Taken his memories, his knowledge, his voice. Some dark magic tied to the Ember itself, perhaps \- a trap for anyone who tried to steal it.

The thought made her sick with rage.

The monster was walking around with pieces of Kaelen inside it. Using his memories to evade capture. Speaking with echoes of his voice.

She would find it. She would cut those pieces out of it. And she would make it suffer for what it had taken.

Within a week, she had assembled a hunting party: Cousin Tomas, four family soldiers, and two hired trackers with experience hunting monsters. They picked up the trail heading northeast, toward the Cyrios Mountains and the border of the Dwendalian Empire.

Sareena rode at the head of the column, her brother's sword strapped to her back alongside her own.

She would carry it until she could return it to his grave, wet with his killer's blood.

### **Part Ten: The Opening State**

When the campaign begins, Kaelen has been running for several weeks. He's made his way far from Port Damali. He's learned to keep his head down, cover his face, avoid questions.

#### ***He's Struggling With:***

* The practicalities of being a hobgoblin in a world that fears them  
* The isolation of losing everyone he loved in a single night  
* The voice at the edge of his thoughts, growing more distinct  
* His oath \- he swore to protect, but what does protection mean when you are the threat?

#### ***He's Holding Onto:***

* His name (even if he can't use it)  
* His training (his paladin abilities remain, proof that his oath recognizes his duty as unbroken)  
* His hope that somehow, someday, he can make this right

*He is exactly where a good story should start: broken but not defeated, lost but not without purpose, alone but carrying something vast inside him.*

## **Korgath Two-Souls**

# **Korgath Two-Souls**

## *Goliath (Fire Giant Heritage) Barbarian \- Path of the Infernal*

# **Birth Among the Cinderborn**

Korgath was born among the **Cinderborn Herd**, a goliath clan dwelling in the volcanic ridges of the **Flotket Alps** in the Greying Wildlands of Wildemount. Unlike most goliath herds who trace their blood to stone giants, the Cinderborn claim descent from an ancient union between their ancestors and fire giants \- those who dwell in the molten depths beneath the **Ashkeeper Peaks** far to the south.

From his first breath, Korgath was marked. His skin bore a deep reddish-ochre hue, threaded with faint blue-grey veins like cooling lava. His eyes were mismatched \- one the color of ember, the other ice-blue. The midwife recoiled, whispering that she saw *two spirits fighting* behind his gaze. The clan's chieftain, **Grothka the Unyielding**, named him **Two-Souls** before he drew his second breath.

He was watched. He was feared. And he was chosen.

# **Childhood: The Outsider Within**

Korgath's childhood was defined by contradiction. He was stronger than other children his age, his temper burned hotter, and when he fought \- even in games \- something *else* seemed to move through him. The other children called him "Ash-Blood" behind his back. The elders watched him with expressions that hovered between reverence and dread.

His only ally was his father, **Morvak Cindervein**, a quiet blacksmith who worked the clan's forge near the volcanic vents. Morvak never spoke of Korgath's mother \- she had died in childbirth, or so he claimed \- but sometimes Korgath caught his father staring at him with an expression he couldn't name. Grief? Guilt? *Fear?*

Morvak taught Korgath the forge. The heat never bothered the boy. He could grip cooling metal that would blister other hands. Morvak called this a "gift." Others called it a warning.

When Korgath was twelve winters old, his father vanished. He left in the night, taking nothing but a pack and a single letter that Korgath found burned to ash in the forge. No body was ever found. No explanation given. The clan elders ruled it a "wanderer's end" \- the goliath term for those who walk into the mountains to die alone.

Korgath never believed it.

# **The Ritual of Sundering**

At sixteen, the clan's shaman \- an ancient goliath named **Sureth Three-Visions** \- approached Korgath with a proposition. The boy's dual nature was unstable, she said. The spirits within him were at war. If left untreated, he would either burn himself out or become something the clan would need to put down.

There was a ritual, she explained. Old. Older than the clan. Older, perhaps, than the mountains themselves. It would *bind* the spirits. Unite them. Make him whole.

Korgath agreed.

The ritual took three days. Sureth carved and inked an elaborate tattoo across Korgath's left shoulder, spiraling down across his chest and stopping directly over his heart. The symbols were not goliath script \- Korgath recognized none of them. They looked *hungry*.

**On the third night, something went wrong.**

The tattoo ignited. Not with ordinary fire \- this was **black-edged flame** that cast no light, only shadow. Korgath screamed, but no sound emerged. He felt something *looking* at him through the pain. Something vast. Something that *smiled*.

When he woke, Sureth was dead \- her body unmarked but her eyes burned hollow from within. The other ritual attendants had fled. Korgath lay alone in the ceremonial cave, the tattoo now dormant but faintly warm against his skin.

He began hearing voices in his dreams. They spoke in a language he shouldn't understand \- but did. They promised him power. They promised him purpose. They promised him *fire enough to remake the world*.

He told no one.

# **Finding Light: Vathru and Brenneth**

Despite the darkness coiling within him, Korgath found something he never expected: love.

**Vathru Stonewake** was a hunter of the clan \- fierce, sharp-tongued, and utterly unimpressed by Korgath's reputation. Where others saw a monster waiting to happen, she saw a man carrying weight he never asked for. She challenged him constantly, mocked his brooding silences, and slowly, relentlessly, pulled him into the light.

They were bonded in the traditional way \- exchanging stone tokens beneath the twin moons, Catha and Ruidus. Later, Korgath would remember that Ruidus seemed to pulse red that night. At the time, he dismissed it.

Two years later, their daughter was born. **Brenneth**. She inherited Korgath's mismatched eyes and Vathru's wild, rasping laugh. She was small for a goliath child \- the elders worried \- but fierce in spirit. Korgath called her "Little Ember."

For six years, he had peace. The voices in his dreams faded to whispers. The tattoo remained dormant. He hunted, he forged, he watched his daughter grow, and he began to believe that perhaps the ritual had worked after all. Perhaps he was just a man now.

He was wrong.

# **Exile**

**Chief Rokan Bloodspear** had watched Korgath for years.

Rokan was a war-leader who had risen to power through cunning and brutality after Grothka the Unyielding died in a rockslide \- a rockslide that some whispered was too convenient. He coveted strength above all things, and he sensed something in Korgath that went beyond muscle and bone. The tattoo. The heritage. The *power* that Korgath himself refused to use.

Rokan wanted it.

He sent envoys first \- offers of position, of honor, of partnership. Korgath refused. The voices had told him what Rokan truly was: a man who would break open the world if he thought it would make him stronger.

When diplomacy failed, Rokan turned to accusation. He declared before the clan that Korgath had murdered Sureth during the ritual \- that the shaman's death was no accident but a deliberate sacrifice to fuel dark power. The elders, cowed by Rokan's cruelty and fearful of Korgath's reputation, did not challenge this narrative.

Korgath was exiled.

# **The Settlement Near Boroftkrah**

Exile, in the end, felt like mercy. Korgath took Vathru and Brenneth far from the Cinderborn's territory, traveling for weeks across the frozen expanse of the **Rime Plains** until they reached a small settlement near **Boroftkrah** \- the orc town at the base of the eastern Flotket Alps where followers of Kord lived in defiance of Gruumsh's shadow.

The settlement was called **Frosthollow** \- a rough collection of hunters, trappers, and outcasts who traded furs and meat with the orcs of Boroftkrah. The locals didn't ask questions about Korgath's past. Strength mattered here. Survival mattered. Everything else was wind and snow.

For three years, they built a life. Korgath worked as a smith and a hunter. Vathru tracked game across the tundra. Brenneth grew wild and fierce and wonderful, playing with the children of orc traders who visited from Boroftkrah. The voices still whispered, but they felt far away \- echoes rather than presence.

Korgath allowed himself to believe it was over.

# **The Betrayal**

The attack came without warning.

Korgath was hunting elk in the high ridges \- three days from Frosthollow \- when the tattoo *screamed* against his chest, blazing with black fire. He felt Vathru's terror like a knife in his gut. He ran. He ran for two days without stopping, the mountains blurring past him, the voice in his head howling with something that might have been rage or might have been *hunger*.

**He arrived too late.**

Frosthollow was ash and silence. The settlement had been razed \- not just his home, but all of it. Bodies lay scattered: human trappers, half-orc traders, the few other goliaths who had settled there. And in the doorway of his home, her hunting spear still clutched in her hand and three arrows in her back, lay Vathru.

She was still breathing. Barely.

Korgath held her as she died. Her last words:

*"He took her... Rokan took her... He spoke of becoming something more..."*

And then:

*"Find your new light... Live for us... Don't stray to the dark. I love you."*

Brenneth was gone. No body. No blood trail. **Taken.**

Among the wreckage, Korgath pieced together what had happened. Rokan had left the Cinderborn \- taken a large force of loyal warriors and struck out on his own. This wasn't clan justice. This was a raid. A *harvest*. Rokan wanted Brenneth \- perhaps because she carried the same fire giant blood as her father. Perhaps because he believed she could be used in whatever dark bargain he was chasing.

In the ruins of his home, Korgath found only one intact possession: a small pendant Vathru had commissioned years ago. A braided metal circle containing tiny painted portraits of Vathru and Brenneth \- mother and daughter, captured in miniature. It should have burned with everything else.

Korgath hung it around his neck, over his heart, over the tattoo.

Then he buried his wife beneath a cairn of frozen stone and began to hunt.

# **The Hunt**

Korgath tracked Rokan's warband south across the Rime Plains, toward the **Quannah Breach** \- the pass through the Flotket Alps that led into the Dwendalian Empire. The trail was days old but clear: a force of twenty or more goliaths moving fast, not bothering to hide their passage.

But as he descended from the mountains into the **Zemni Fields**, the trail scattered. Rokan had split his forces, sent false trails in multiple directions. The warband vanished like smoke in wind.

For three months, Korgath searched. He took mercenary work where he could find it \- protection jobs, monster hunting \- anything that let him keep moving and paid for information. He followed rumors of goliath raiders. He questioned travelers who had crossed the Empire. He learned that Rokan's name was spoken in whispers among those who dealt in dark things.

The trail led him north, toward the frozen coast. Toward a port town where information flowed like the ice-choked waters of the **Frigid Depths**.

# **Arrival at Icehaven**

**Icehaven** clings to the northern coast of the Dwendalian Empire like a desperate survivor in a tempest. The sole port in Western Wynandir, this frost-bitten settlement harbors the few ships brave enough to navigate the treacherous ice floes of the Frigid Depths. Hunters, trappers, sailors, and those seeking passage to the frozen wastes of **Eiselcross** all pass through its cramped, snow-covered streets.

Korgath arrived as winter tightened its grip.   
Icehaven is a crossroads. Travelers from across the Empire pass through. Sailors who brave the Frigid Depths carry stories from distant shores. If anyone has seen Rokan's warband \- if anyone knows where they're heading \- Korgath will find them here.

And somewhere in the frozen dark, his daughter is waiting.

\---

*Brenneth is alive. She has to be alive.*

*Everything else is secondary.*

### **Part I · The Debt**

Rudeus Greyt never knew his mother. What he knew was Shadycreek Run, the lawless city in the Greying Wildlands where coin is the only law and the criminal families rule everything. And what he knew was his father: a deadbeat who could barely keep them fed, a man who drifted through life accumulating debts he never meant to pay.

The debts were owed to the Jagentoth family. Nothing dramatic, no grand vice, just the slow arithmetic of a life lived beyond its means. Borrowed coin for food, for shelter, for whatever scheme was finally going to turn things around. It never did. The debt only grew. And when his father died (sickness, neglect, it hardly mattered which), it passed to the only thing he left behind.

His son.

By twelve, Rudy was running jobs for the family: deliveries, lookout work, anything to keep the interest from compounding. By sixteen he was muscle. By nineteen the debt had somehow tripled despite years of service, and he had learned the lesson every debtor in Shadycreek eventually learns: you are not meant to pay it off. You are meant to be owned.

He never wanted trouble. He only wanted to work off what he owed and be free. But freedom wasn't profitable, and his bosses had noticed the way certain buyers looked at their workforce: the strong ones, the young ones, the ones nobody would miss. Why squeeze a lifetime of labor out of a debtor when you could sell him outright for coin today?

The buyers came robed, paying in platinum, speaking of someone they called The Avarice, the Coin Mistress, with a reverence that bordered on worship, as if she were a demigod and not a mortal. Rudy never saw her. He saw only the faceless ones who came for him: black robes where men should be, and veils of shifting smoke curling where their eyes and mouths should have been.

They paid well. The family handed him over without a second thought. Another asset liquidated. Another line cleared from the books.

---

### **Part II · The Faceless**

They took him to a village called Yardel, deep in the Marrow Valley in the foothills of the Dunrock Mountains. Above ground it was a husk \- boarded cabins, thin streets, a place that had clearly lost something it never got back. A few elven families remained, what was left of an older community, but they kept their heads down like everyone else. Coin had bought the village's silence years before he arrived. Coin, it seemed, bought everything. The Coin Mistress's reach was long.

The facility lay beneath the village: chambers carved into ancient stone and lit by cold arcane light. Rudy was not the only one brought down there. Dozens filled the cages, debtors, orphans, prisoners, anyone who wouldn't be missed, kept like animals in a menagerie while the faceless figures went about their work.

The work was blood magic. They were trying to replace the blood in their subjects' bodies with other things: other essences, other natures. They brought in creatures Rudy had never seen, twisted things that screamed in languages that hurt to hear. Some subjects went to the tables and came back changed. Others went and never came back at all. Rudy watched from his cage, learning to tell which footsteps meant feeding and which meant someone would simply be gone by morning.

When they came for him, they strapped him to a table beside something demonic, a thing of shadow and sinew that thrashed against its bindings in a fury that looked almost like terror. The instruments came down. Pain was the first thing, white and total, his screams tangling with the creature's. He didn't understand what was being done to him. He only knew that something was being drawn out of the thing on the next table and threaded into him, into his veins, like liquid fire.

He woke in his cage. The creature was gone. Dead, consumed, or discarded, he never knew. But he was different. He felt it in his bones, in the unfamiliar energy twitching through his muscles. When he looked at his hands and willed it, his fingers lengthened and his nails blackened into claws; his reflection in the water bucket showed a face that warped into something animal, something other, riding just beneath his skin.

He could change. Become faster, sharper, deadlier: not a beast of fur and fang but a thing of demonic shadow, wrong and right at once. The essence had fused with him.

The sessions continued. More tables, more pain. But now they taught him, too: how to command the blood, how to pull on the crimson tide that runs through every living thing. Slowly Rudy understood. They weren't only changing him. They were forging him. Into a weapon.

Not everyone in the cages saw it as horror. There was a man named Vesren, arrived around the same time, and where Rudy felt dread when the robes came, Vesren felt rapture. He welcomed the tables. He marveled at what his body was becoming. He spoke of the Faceless and their Coin Mistress with genuine devotion. To him the pain was a gift and the transformation an elevation, the making of something greater out of a man who had been nothing.

Vesren attached himself to Rudy early, certain he'd found a kindred soul. He was wrong, but Rudy never corrected him; it was easier to let him believe. They trained together, worked together, and Vesren called it friendship. Rudy thought of it more as two men assigned the same task.

But Vesren was dangerous in a way that had nothing to do with claws. He was a true believer, and true believers report heresy. More than once Rudy watched fellow prisoners vanish after Vesren "expressed concerns" about their loyalty. So Rudy learned to bury his doubts deep, to mirror the fanatic's enthusiasm on command, and to never let the mask slip, because he had seen what happened to those who did.

### **Part III · The Hound**

In the end, he stopped resisting.

What was the point? His life had always been this: following orders, serving someone else, being used. At least now he was useful. At least now he had a purpose, even one chosen for him. He set down whatever was left of his own will and did as he was told, because it was easier, and it had always been easier.

For five years, Rudeus Greyt was their soldier.

They sent him across Wildemount on errands that served ends he was never told. Killings in crowded markets. Thefts from guarded vaults. Distractions to move other agents unseen. The taking of people with rare talents or bloodlines the Faceless wanted for their tables. The hunting of those who tried to run. Rudy did all of it, and he was good at it: the shift made him fast, the blood magic made him lethal, and the hollow place inside him made him reliable. He didn't ask questions. He didn't hesitate. He was their hound, and a hound doesn't think. It obeys.

Until Yara.

### **Part IV · The Spider**

It was supposed to be a simple retrieval. It wasn't.

The target lived in Berleben, a rot-and-stilts swamp town in the Marrow Valley where the streets were boardwalks and the air tasted of standing water. A woman. The Faceless had wanted her for years. Rudy gathered that much from how little they told him. They didn't say why she mattered. He suspected they didn't know. He only knew that others had been sent before him, that none of those others had come back, and that this time they were sending their best hound. Bring her in alive. That was all.

Her name was Yara.

She looked like no one. That was the first thing. Nothing about her explained why anyone would burn years and bodies trying to collect her; no glow, no mark, no weight that a stranger would notice. Short dark hair she had plainly cut herself, cropped close and shaved at the nape. A sharp, quick, pretty face that never stopped moving, usually caught somewhere mid-grin. Lean and wiry, faster than she looked. The eyes were the only tell: even laughing, even drunk, they were counting the exits and clocking every stranger in the room. And her hands didn't match the rest of her: calluses and small white scars, a thief's hands and a killer's hands turning out to be the same hands. A worn leather cord sat at her throat with an old charm hung from it, riding a pale stripe it had worn into her skin over a lifetime of never coming off.

She wasn't hiding. That threw him. He had expected a frightened woman keeping her head down; instead he found someone who worked the town like she owned it: lifting purses, running little cons on the barge traders, drinking too much and laughing too loud and walking away from games of chance heavier than she'd sat down. In the wrong circles she had a name, though never the same one twice. She was reckless and magnetic and clearly enjoying herself, and Rudy, who had spent five years being pointed at people, did not know what to make of a target who seemed to be having the time of her life.

She noticed the new laborer on the docks the way she noticed everything, and she decided he was interesting. She didn't fuss over him. She dared him. The second night she pulled him off the boardwalk and onto a moored barge to lift a crate of southern wine, grinning back at him the whole time, daring him to be quieter, faster, braver. And afterward, sitting in the reeds drinking someone else's wine straight from the bottle, she looked at the skinny dangerous stranger who ate like a stray and never expected seconds, and asked him, half a tease and half a real question:

*"Are you hungry?"*

Like it was a dare. Like she already knew the answer and only wanted to see what he'd do with something he hadn't earned and couldn't pay back.

He didn't have an answer. He had been a tool his whole life: pointed, fired, put away. No one had ever invited him to simply take a thing because he wanted it. She did it again and again over the days that followed: pulled him into small crimes and pointless adventures, treated him not as a project to fix but as a co-conspirator, an equal, someone worth daring. She was plainly more dangerous than he was and not remotely afraid of him, and for Rudy that was the most intoxicating thing that had ever happened. She made his small, gray, caged life feel suddenly, dizzyingly enormous.

He learned what she truly was on the fourth night, and it took about four seconds.

They were laughing about nothing when a man at the end of the dock went still in the way that means recognition. Yara finished her sentence. Walked over. What she did was quick and ugly and over before Rudy fully registered it, and then she was back beside him, wiping her hands on her skirt, picking the conversation up exactly where she'd dropped it. The part that would follow him the rest of his life was this: he, a man the Faceless had built specifically to do that, was the one who flinched. She didn't become someone else to do it. There was no monster hiding under the girl. It was all one person, and she saw nothing in it worth slowing down for. He understood then that she wasn't hiding from anything. She was the most dangerous thing in this town, and she had been letting him think otherwise because it amused her.

They talked, those nights, pieces of the truth traded in the dark. It started, for him, as the oldest trick in his trade: a spy builds trust by giving a little of himself away. But somewhere the giving stopped being a trick. He told her about Shadycreek and the debt that had never been his, about the table and the thing they had burned into his blood, about five years of doing as he was told because being told was easier than wanting. He had never said any of it aloud. She listened like it cost her nothing and meant everything. And she gave him her own shape of it: that she had been hunted since she was a small girl, by faces made of smoke, and that in twenty years of running she had never once learned why. She didn't know what she was. She only knew she was wanted, and that the wanting had cost her every place she'd ever tried to call home.

They were not the same kind of creature. But they were the same kind of thing: two people who had only ever been something other people owned, or wanted to.

His days ran out. There was a date by which he was meant to deliver her, and after it the Faceless would know their hound had slipped its leash. He stood at the edge of the only real choice he had ever been allowed and found it was the hardest thing in the world: the one person who had ever looked at him and seen a man, against the only way of surviving he knew.

She made it easier and worse at the same time. On the last night she told him, lightly, smiling, in the middle of something gentle, that she had known what he was the moment he set foot on her dock. She'd known they were coming. She always knew. She had stayed in Berleben on purpose, to deal on her own terms with whatever they sent. He had never been the hunter. He had been a mouse in the spider's house the entire time, watched and weighed and allowed to live because she had wanted to see whether there was anybody home behind the hound's eyes. The "are you hungry" had been, in part, a test. And it had also been real. Which part was which, whether anything she felt was true or just a game that had run past its own edges, she didn't say, and Rudy came to understand that she didn't know either, and never would. Neither would he. That not-knowing was the thing he'd carry north.

He told her he wouldn't take her. He told her to run, that when he failed to report, they would send something worse than him. But the worse problem was his own blood: the Faceless could find him by it the instant he turned, scry the essence in his veins like a lit lantern, and then they would have them both.

So she took the cord from her throat. The charm on it blocked exactly that kind of finding. It was how she had stayed invisible for twenty years, how the smoke-faces had only ever caught her trail the slow, mundane way, by rumor and by a face someone recognized. There was only one. She had taken it off the first hunter she ever killed, when she was still a child, and she had not removed it since; the pale stripe on her neck was suddenly bare, like a wound. She pressed it into the hand of the hound who wasn't supposed to matter, and gave away the only thing that had ever kept her safe.

He asked her to come with him. North, somewhere cold and quiet, somewhere they could both just stop. She laughed, not unkindly. They both knew the truth of it: two hunted things in one place is a beacon, and besides, she had no interest in stopping. He was running toward quiet. She was a shark; she would die if she went still, and she had spent her whole life refusing to be owned by anyone or anything: a debt, an order, a town, a man. Disappearing into a warm little life with him would only be a prettier cage, and she was allergic to cages. Maybe she was still running at the thing that hunted her, chasing an answer no one alive could give her. Either way, it wasn't north.

They had seen each other completely, more than anyone ever had, and still they couldn't go the same way.

*"Never take it off,"* she said, of the charm. And then, the last thing:

*"You have a choice now. Make it count."*

They parted at dawn. She went one way, bright and dangerous and gone. He went the other.

### **Part V · Icehaven**

He chose north, as far north as a man could walk, to where the cold might smother whatever the charm couldn't. He kept off the main roads, hunted his own food, slept in ditches and caves and the lee of frozen rock, and after weeks of it he came to Icehaven.

Icehaven is a frontier town, the kind of place people come to disappear. Fishermen, traders, fugitives, fortune-hunters: everyone there has a past they'd rather not discuss, and asking questions is the worst kind of manners. For Rudy it is as close to sanctuary as he is likely to find. He has been here three weeks. He's found work on the docks: hauling crates, mending nets, the labor that wants a strong back and no references. He keeps his head down. He doesn't make friends. He sleeps with a blade under his pillow and one eye open, and the charm never leaves his throat.

The shift is still coiled inside him like a second heartbeat. The blood magic still answers when he calls it. He is still the weapon they made. But he is a weapon without a hand to wield him now, and the freedom of that frightens him nearly as much as the Faceless ever did. He has wanted nothing, his whole life, beyond surviving the next order. He does not yet know how to want anything else. He's learning. It's slow.

He thinks about Yara. Not whether she's safe. She is the most dangerous person he has ever met, and the thought of anyone cornering her is almost funny. He wonders where she is. What she's running at. Whether the thing she has chased her whole life will ever turn around and give her a name for itself. Whether any of those nights in the reeds were real to her, or whether he was only ever the most interesting job she'd had in a while. He'll never know. He has made his peace with never knowing. Mostly.

Some nights he catches himself doing something he was never built to do: taking the long way home because he likes the look of the ice, pocketing an apple off a cart just to feel that he could, laughing at something with no one around to perform for. Small, reckless, alive. She'd have liked that, he thinks. That's how he knows it mattered.

For the first time in his life, Rudeus Greyt belongs to no one. He doesn't know what kind of man he wants to be. He has never had the luxury of wanting. But he is starting to find out. One choice at a time.

## **Player 6 \- Nah’lor**

*Nah’lor*  
*Species: Mandrake   Druid  Circle of Dragons but we reflavored it to phoenix*  
*Level 1*

**NAH'LOR**

*Circle of the Phoenix Druid*

A Unique Creature of Bark, Ember, and Dreams

*"Names are seeds. They grow into their meaning. Ask me again in a hundred years."*

*— Miztik Flitterbit*

**PART ONE: THE BACKSTORY**

**Chapter 1: The Dead Forest**

**The Blighted Land**

She came into awareness in a place of wrongness. A forest of wicker-black trees, twisted and lifeless. No birdsong. No wind through leaves. A scar on the world that stretched in every direction. Why the forest died, what killed it, how long it had been this way: unknown. Just endless black and twisted wood in every direction.

Her grove grew in the heart of this desolation: the only faint nature left. A small pocket of green clinging to existence in a sea of death. Why her grove survived when nothing else did is a mystery she carries. But even this small sanctuary was dying. Barely any sustenance from staying rooted. She conjured goodberries by instinct, a magic she performed without understanding, but it was never enough.

**Existence Before Uprooting**

Stationary. Watching. Learning without understanding. Years passed while she observed. The dead forest still had desperate life clinging to it: night-like creatures locked in brutal cycles over scarce resources. They fought for anything. For life. They ate each other. They ate from her small grove, which produced very little sustenance. She witnessed the circle of life in its harshest form. Nothing was wasted. Everything fought. Death fed life fed death.

She didn't judge this. It simply was. Her first framework for understanding the world. She learned patience, stillness, and how to endure. She understood that death is a state, not an ending, just part of the cycle. The shape of absence became familiar to her; she understood loss before she understood having.

**The Elven Girl**

One day, a traveler passed through the dead place. An elven girl, pursued by something. One of the creatures of the dead forest, or something worse. She fought. She won. She was wounded but alive.

This was the first time the young grove-creature saw something choose to survive rather than simply react. The girl left, or died later, or vanished into the black trees. But her image stayed. Strength. Defiance. A shape worth becoming.

**The Uprooting**

A year later. A desperate bear-like predator, starving, saw her grove as sustenance. It attacked. Wounded her before she could react. In the moment of threat, instinct took over.

She pulled herself from the earth, roots tearing free, and her form shifted. When she stood, she wore the shape of the elven girl: bark-skinned, leaf-touched, but recognizably humanoid. Recognizably dangerous. The bear-creature hesitated. That hesitation saved her.

She fled. Her first steps. The wound bled sap. She wandered, weakening, sustained only by goodberries. The grove behind her was truly dying now without her presence. She couldn't go back. The attack was the final push, but she would have had to leave eventually or wither away with the last remnants of life in that dead place.

**Chapter 2: The Tear**

**First Light**

Wounded and wandering, she saw it. A wound in the air, edges crackling with energy, light spilling through like water. Creatures fleeing through it, panicked, desperate. They didn't even see her.

She had seen creatures flee before in the dead forest, but never like this. Never toward light. Never toward something that felt like hope. What were they running from? Where were they going? She didn't know. But the light called to her.

Curiosity. Desperation. Hope she didn't have a name for yet. She stepped through.

**The Doralle Woods**

The portal deposited her somewhere in the Doralle Woods of Wildemount. Light filtered through living canopy for the first time. Green so vivid it almost hurt. She collapsed. Rooted instinctively. Drank in the sun.

The wounds began to close. Not fast, but steadily. The sun was feeding her in a way goodberries never could. For the first time, she felt at home. As if the sun rays themselves were giving her life. Hours passed. Maybe a full day. She simply existed in the warmth.

When she finally moved again, she was different. More aware. More curious. This new world was the first time she felt curious about nature itself, not just what happened on its surface. She wanted to understand everything.

**Chapter 3: Miztik Flitterbit**

**The Fey Who Found Her**

A spriggan: small, goblin-sized, with butterfly-like wings that shimmer faintly. Green-blue skin, eyes too bright, smile too wide. He found her while she basked in the sunlight, still healing, still confused.

He sensed something in her immediately. Not just the shape, but something deeper. An ancient flame core wrapped in bark. A walking tree with magic he'd never seen before. A species of one. He found this fascinating. What is she? What will she become?

**What He Taught Her**

Common language. Druidic. That she could shape the magic already inside her. Basic social concepts: names, greetings, the idea that not everything is predator or prey. How to move through a forest without disturbing it.

But he didn't want to shape her. He wanted to see what she would become on her own. He was more curious about what HER choices would be rather than dictating for her what to do. He gave her tools, not answers. When she asked why she felt certain things, he'd tilt his head and say, 'Interesting question. Why do you think?'

**The Name: Nah'lor**

He gave her the name Nah'lor without explaining it. When she asked what it meant, he just grinned with too many teeth and said, 'It's what you are until you're something else.'

Possible meanings he would never confirm: something in an old fey tongue. A corruption of an elvish phrase. A piece of a true name that will become complete when she does. Just sounds he liked. The truth is probably all of these and none of them.

**The Gift**

Before he sent her toward civilization, he pressed a seed into her hand. Small, dark, warm to the touch no matter the weather.

*"Keep this. You'll know what to do with it someday. Or you won't. Either way, it's yours now."*

She later made it into a necklace, using a cord she got in Gwardan. It rests against her chest, near where her heartwood would be. What it is, what it does, whether it connects them somehow: she doesn't know. He wouldn't tell her.

**The Sending Off**

He pointed her toward Gwardan, the elven city south of the Doralle Woods. 'They're elves, like the shape you wear. Go. Learn. Come back and tell me what you find.' He didn't tell her what to do there. He just wanted to see what would happen.

**Chapter 4: Gwardan**

**Arrival**

She walked out of the Doralle Woods toward the fields surrounding Gwardan. Her appearance drew attention: bark skin, leaves at her extremities, fey-elven features. Strange, but the people of Gwardan had dealt with fey-touched things before. Unusual, not unheard of.

She spoke Common haltingly. She didn't understand money. She wasn't wearing clothes. That last part caused some problems. Miztik probably found it funnier than he should have.

**Learning to Be a Person**

A red panda humanoid named Sha'reh took her in. Sha'reh was an herbalist who traded with the Doralle Woods, a fey creature with soft russet fur, a ringed tail, and kind dark eyes. Like Nah'lor, Sha'reh had come through a portal connecting the Feywild to Exandria. She told Nah'lor stories about her home there, the strange beauty of it, the way time moved differently. She knew exactly what she was talking about. She invited Nah'lor to come visit someday. Nah'lor was curious about this.

Sha'reh taught her the basics: clothing (why it matters to people), money exchange (coins have value, work earns coins), the concept of work itself, gods and deities (powerful beings that people worship, though Nah'lor found this strange). She gave Nah'lor a place to stay while she learned.

Despite all this learning, Nah'lor remained fairly naive. She understood she created attention around her appearance, yet didn't try too hard to hide herself. She grasped the basics of common sense but still struggled with the subtleties of social interaction.

**Friends**

Beyond Sha'reh, Nah'lor made other connections in Gwardan. Martuf, a young gnome student at the House of Invention, became her first real friend outside of Sha'reh. Excitable and curious, he explained what adventurers were, showed her books about mythical creatures, and taught her that questions are valuable.

Elder Vaemis, an elderly elf cleric at the House of Enchantment, sensed something unusual in her but didn't push. He explained gods and faith in terms she could understand. And Rynn, a young elf girl who lived near Sha'reh's shop, treated Nah'lor like a wonder rather than a threat, teaching her games, laughter, and simple words for emotions.

**The Woodcarving**

She discovered she could carve. Using branches grown from her own druidic magic or wood gathered from nature, she shaped small statues of things she'd seen. Animals. Trees. Faces. People liked them. They paid coin for them. This became her way of surviving in Gwardan.

She carved what she witnessed, what she remembered, what she dreamed. Most dreams were nothing: blurs of color, sensations of heat, gone by morning.

**Chapter 5: The Dream**

**The Vision**

One night, a dream came that was different from all the others. Longer. Clearer. Unforgettable.

A night sky. Three moons hanging in the darkness. A creature of impossible beauty soaring through the air. Not made of fire exactly. Made of twilight. Cyan and purple flames that didn't burn but shimmered like aurora.

It moved like water, like wind, like something between life and dream. She watched it in awe. She felt what it felt: freedom, age, weariness, and something like longing. It said nothing. It didn't need to. She understood.

She woke with warmth in her chest and the faint smell of smoke.

**The Carving**

She carved what she saw. Her hands knew the shape before her mind did. A bird of flame, wings spread, feathers like tongues of fire.

She showed it to people in Gwardan. No one recognized it exactly. But they told her stories. 'That looks like something from the old tales. A firebird. A sun-crane. The kind of thing adventurers claim to see in far places.'

They told her about wondrous creatures. Beings of magic and myth that most people never encounter. She realized: she needed to find what she dreamed. She needed to understand why it called to her.

She decided to become someone who seeks such things. An adventurer.

**Chapter 6: The Trap**

**Unwanted Attention**

Not everyone in Gwardan had kind intentions. Veleth Marr, a trader with connections to The Galleria, noticed Nah'lor's unique nature. Smooth, polite, patient, he started asking questions. Offered to buy her carvings at prices that were too high. Really wanted to know where she came from, what she was.

He saw profit in her. Her exotic appearance could fetch a fortune to the right buyer. His hired hands had tried to grab her before, in an alley, but she escaped. They were relentless. They kept watching, kept planning.

**What Happened**

They set a trap at a place they knew she frequented. But when they sprung it, Nah'lor wasn't there.

Sha'reh was.

They took her anyway. Spite. A message. Maybe leverage for later. The red panda woman who had shown Nah'lor nothing but kindness, taken because of her.

**The Aftermath**

Nah'lor escaped. Sha'reh didn't. The Zhelezo investigated, but Veleth Marr has connections. Nothing stuck. Sha'reh simply 'disappeared.'

Martuf was devastated. He told Nah'lor it wasn't her fault, but something in his eyes said otherwise.

Elder Vaemis quietly suggested she leave. 'You draw attention that others pay for. Go. Find what you're looking for. Perhaps you'll find her too.'

Rynn didn't understand why Sha'reh was gone or why Nah'lor was leaving. She just cried and gave her the carved wooden bird she'd been working on. A crane.

**What This Means**

Guilt she doesn't fully know how to process. Sha'reh helped her. Sha'reh was taken because of her. A debt she doesn't know how to repay. She has to find Sha'reh. Somehow.

Her first real understanding that her existence has consequences for others. A reason to keep moving. Staying anywhere too long puts people in danger.

A name to remember: Veleth Marr. The Galleria. Someday there will be a reckoning.

**Chapter 7: The Pull North**

**Rumors of Icehaven**

After leaving Gwardan, Nah'lor travels. She's looking for wondrous creatures, for answers about her dream, for any sign of Sha'reh. Along the way, she hears rumors:

* A place called Icehaven in the Dwendalian Empire, a port town where strange things arrive.

* Fantastical creatures arriving by boats, carried from distant lands.

* People going missing there. Kidnappings reported.

* Adventurers gathering, seeking fortune and mystery.

**Why Icehaven?**

Fantastical creatures. The kind from the old stories. The kind that might know about what she dreamed. If wondrous beings arrive there by boat, perhaps she can find answers about the twilight phoenix.

People go missing there. Kidnappings. Maybe that's where people like Sha'reh end up. Maybe there's a trail to follow.

People who seek kidnapped victims and hunt fantastical creatures are adventurers. This is what adventurers do. She wants to be one. Here's her first real purpose.

And so she journeys north, into Empire territory. Toward Icehaven. Toward answers. Toward Sha'reh.
