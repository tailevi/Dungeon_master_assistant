Backstory  
Ermac the Shadow Walker  
Pallid Elf Druid (Circle of Shadows)  
The Navajo Family  
The Navajo family was one of the older bloodlines in the Pallid Grove, descended from moon priests who had helped guide their people through the dark centuries following the Calamity. Ermac (born 745 PD) and his younger sister Narsett (born 750 PD) grew up in a household that honored both the old nature traditions and the protective shadow magic that had become part of their heritage.  
Childhood in the Pallid Grove (750s-800s PD)  
The Trio Forms  
When Ferol Sal arrived in their lives around 758 PD (age 5), the dynamic of Ermac and Navajo's sibling relationship changed completely. The three became an inseparable trio, each bringing something unique to their friendship:  
Ermac (13 when they all met) was the steady older brother \- patient, wise beyond his years, and naturally gifted with shadow druidry. He served as the group's conscience and often mediated between his more impulsive companions.  
Narsett (8 when they met) was spirited, curious, and surprisingly brave for her age. Where Ermac was contemplative, she was action-oriented. She had inherited the family's moon magic but showed unusual aptitude for enchantment and illusion.  
Ferol (5 when they met) was the youngest but often seemed the most mature intellectually. His questions about magic and the world beyond the grove both fascinated and sometimes disturbed his older friends.  
Narsett's Growing Feelings  
As they grew older, Narsett developed a deep crush on Ferol that evolved into genuine love. She was drawn to his brilliance, his ambition, and yes \- even his darker interests. While Ermac worried about Ferol's fascination with necromancy, Narsett found it thrilling and exotic.  
The Necromancy Incident Revisited: When Ermac discovered 17-year-old Ferol practicing necromancy (and Narsett, now 15, helping him), the confrontation was even more devastating. Narsett defended Ferol passionately, arguing that knowledge shouldn't be forbidden simply because others feared it. This created the first serious rift between the siblings.  
Narsett's Secret Studies: After the incident, while Ferol was confined, Narsett secretly continued practicing the techniques they'd learned together. She became skilled at hiding her necromantic abilities behind illusion magic, presenting a mask of acceptable moon-touched enchantment to her family.  
Key Childhood Moments  
The Shadow Garden Pact: When the three friends reconciled after the necromancy incident, they modified their pact. Ermac and Ferol's original promise was expanded to include Narsett's vow to "protect the bonds between them no matter where their paths led." All three contributed to the shadow moss garden, intertwining their magical essences in a way that would let them sense each other across great distances.  
Narsett's Champion: Throughout their teens, Narsett consistently defended Ferol's more controversial interests. When elders criticized his questions about Aeor or his magical experiments, she would redirect attention or provide alibis. Ermac watched this with growing unease, seeing how his sister's loyalty was becoming obsession.  
The Love Confession: When Narsett was 45 and Ferol 43, she finally confessed her feelings. Ferol, focused on his studies and plans to leave the grove, gently but firmly rejected her romantic advances. He valued their friendship too much to risk it with romance, and he knew his path would be dangerous. This devastated Narsett but also strengthened her resolve to prove herself worthy of his ambitions.  
The Departure (Around 800 PD)  
When Ferol left for Rexxentrum, both Ermac and their family expected Narsett to remain in the grove. She had given no indication of leaving, and their parents had made it clear they disapproved of Ferol's dangerous ambitions.  
The Disappearance: Three days after Ferol's departure, Narsett vanished without a trace. She left only a hastily written note: "I've gone to be with Ferol. Don't follow me. I'm finally free." The family was devastated \- they had expected her to grieve Ferol's departure and then move on, not abandon everything to chase after him.  
Ermac's Immediate Concern: From the moment Ermac read that note, anxiety gnawed at him. The magical bond he shared with his sister felt strained and distant. He knew she was alive, but something felt wrong \- as if she was farther away than mere distance could explain.  
The Letters Begin (800-825 PD)  
The First Letter: About a month after Narsett's disappearance, the first letter arrived. It was excited, almost manic in tone. She had found Ferol, they were together, and they were "doing important work" that would "change everything." But reading between the lines, Ermac noticed disturbing details \- references to "older techniques" and "ancient knowledge" that shouldn't have been available to academy students.  
Growing Concern: Each subsequent letter deepened Ermac's worry. Narsett wrote about experiments that grew increasingly dark, about Ferol's "true genius" being far beyond what anyone in the grove had imagined. Her handwriting changed over time \- becoming more erratic, fevered.  
Troubling Revelations: Around 810 PD, Narsett's letters began mentioning things that didn't add up. She spoke of Ferol referencing events and techniques that predated their time in the grove. She mentioned his "true age" and how "he's shown me what really happened during the Calamity." These letters filled Ermac with a growing dread that his childhood friend was not who he claimed to be.  
The Final Letters (820-825 PD): Her last few letters were different \- shorter, vaguer, and strangely formal. She mentioned they were conducting "important research in a remote location" and that communication might become irregular. The last letter simply said: "We're on the verge of something incredible. Don't worry if you don't hear from me for a while. The work requires complete focus."  
Ermac's Investigation (825-834 PD)  
When the letters stopped, Ermac immediately began his own investigation. Using his shadow-walking abilities and connections throughout the grove's network, he started uncovering disturbing truths.  
The Age Discrepancy: Through painstaking research, Ermac discovered records that didn't match. Birth records, witness accounts, and magical signatures all pointed to the same impossible conclusion \- Ferol was far older than he had claimed. References to someone matching his exact description appeared in records dating back to the Calamity itself.  
The Aeorian Connection: Following the trail, Ermac learned that Ferol hadn't just studied Aeorian magic \- he had been Aeorian. An original necromancer from the flying city, someone who had survived its fall through dark magic and had been living in various disguises for over a thousand years.  
The Deception: The crushing realization hit Ermac: their entire childhood friendship had been a lie. Ferol had infiltrated the Pallid Grove not as a refugee child, but as an ancient undead being seeking something specific. The question that haunted Ermac as he journeyed to Eiselcross was: what had Ferol needed from their people, and how did Narsett fit into his ancient plans?  
The Horrific Discovery (834-835 PD)  
When Ermac finally found Salsvault, he witnessed horrors beyond his imagination. Living subjects twisted into abominations, diseases that transformed people into ice statues, and chambers full of failed experiments. The necromantic corruption was so thick he could taste it in the air.  
The Confrontation: When he finally found Ferol in Salsvault \- now revealed as an ancient wight who had lived since the Calamity \- Ermac felt his world shatter. The friend he had grown up with was a lie. An Aeorian necromancer over a thousand years old who had posed as a child to infiltrate their community.  
The Terrible Questions: "What did you need from us?" Ermac demanded. "What did you take from the grove? And where is my sister?"  
Ferol's response was chillingly casual: "Oh, dear Narsett. Always so devoted, so willing to help. She made such valuable contributions to my research. Just like her people's unique connection to shadow magic \- so useful for my work."  
The Imprisonment: When Ermac pressed for answers about where his sister was and what Ferol had really wanted from the Pallid Grove, Ferol's demeanor shifted from nostalgic to coldly practical. With eerie calm, he trapped Ermac in a specially warded cage, explaining that he could use the company while he worked. "Besides," Ferol added with a twisted smile, "you have the same bloodline capabilities as dear Narsett. Waste not, want not."  
The Mystery: Ferol never directly states what happened to Narsett. Sometimes he speaks of her in past tense, other times as if she might return from an errand at any moment. He shows Ermac around his various laboratories, pointing out innovations that "Narsett and I developed together" or equipment that "bears her particular touch." Whether she's dead, transformed into something else, or hidden somewhere in Salsvault remains uncertain.  
Current Situation  
Ermac has been imprisoned for several months, subjected to experiments while desperately trying to learn Narsett's fate. Ferol treats him almost like a pet \- useful company that can appreciate his work but contained enough not to interfere. The worst part is how Ferol occasionally shows flashes of their old friendship, speaking warmly of memories that now feel tainted by what he's become.  
Additional Motivations  
Beyond saving Narsett, Ermac now grapples with several disturbing questions:

What did Ferol specifically need from the Pallid Grove's people or magic?  
How many other communities has Ferol infiltrated over the centuries?  
Are there other survivors of his manipulations?  
What aspects of Pallid Elf shadow magic made them valuable to his experiments?

Goals and Motivations  
Primary Goal: Find out what happened to Narsett and, if possible, save her or at least give her peace.  
Secondary Goals:

Uncover the truth about what Ferol took from the Pallid Grove  
Stop Ferol's horrific experiments  
Expose the deception to protect other communities from similar infiltration  
Prevent the spread of Frigid Woe and other diseases  
Return to warn their people about the ancient danger that lived among them

Emotional Stakes  
Ermac is haunted by guilt \- he failed to save both his sister and his best friend. He wonders if he could have prevented this tragedy by being more supportive of their ambitions or more forceful in his opposition. The uncertainty about Narsett's fate may be worse than knowing she's dead, as his imagination conjures increasingly horrific possibilities.  
Why He'll Join the Party: Beyond gratitude for rescue, Ermac desperately needs allies to search Salsvault thoroughly for any trace of his sister. His knowledge of both Ferol's psychology and the facility's layout makes him invaluable to stopping the spread of Frigid Woe, while his personal stakes ensure he'll never give up the fight. The revelation that his entire childhood friendship was a centuries-long deception adds another layer of motivation \- he needs to understand what Ferol took from his people and ensure it never happens again.

Backstory  
THE BIRTH OF A SEER-WARRIOR  
In the deepest heart of winter, when the auroras painted the ice-cliffs in ethereal light and the wind's howl echoed through ancient caves, a child was born to the Bloodmaw Clan. The birth itself was marked by strange omens \- the eternal flames in the gathering halls flickered blue, and the preserved flesh in the sacred chambers seemed to pulse with an inner light. The Wormtongue Council gathered immediately, and after three days of meditation and ritual, they named the infant Haldrik Bjornskar.

The child's mother, a respected hunter named Yeshra, noted early that her son's eyes held an unusual quality \- a depth that seemed to reflect the ancient ice itself. His father, Korrax Bjornskar, a decorated warrior who had led many successful raids, watched with pride as even as a toddler, Haldrik showed natural balance and strength.

CHILDHOOD IN THE MAW  
Unlike other children who feared the deeper tunnels of their home, young Haldrik was drawn to them. While his peers played at war games in the upper chambers, he would sit for hours in the twisting passages of the Maw of Winter, where the wind created strange songs through the ice formations. Other children whispered that he was touched by the cold, but Varrax the Ancient saw something more.  
The elderly lorekeeper began finding excuses to have the boy present during important ceremonies. During the telling of clan histories, while other children dozed or fidgeted, haldrik sat entranced, his eyes reflecting the ceremonial flames as ancient tales of battles and revelations filled the chamber. He showed an uncanny ability to remember every detail, every ritual phrase, every sacred gesture.

THE FIRST TASTE  
On his thirteenth birthday, during the sacred First Taste ceremony, something unprecedented occurred. As the preserved flesh touched his lips in the ceremonial chamber, Haldrik's eyes rolled back, and he began speaking in an ancient battle-cant that even Varrax had only heard in the oldest ritual chants. The words echoed through the ice chambers, causing the ceremonial flames to dance wildly. For several minutes, he spoke of battles long past and visions of ice and flesh, before collapsing into a deep sleep that lasted three days.

THE WARRIOR'S PATH  
After the dramatic events of his First Taste ceremony, Haldrik's training took a unique turn. While most young warriors focused solely on combat, he was marked for a dual path. His mornings began before dawn with Thokk Icevein's brutal combat training in the outer rings of the Maw. The massive warrior drove Haldrik and other initiates mercilessly through the snow, teaching them to fight in the harshest conditions imaginable.

Thokk's training methods were legendary for their brutality \- young warriors were sometimes buried in snow for hours to learn endurance, or made to climb sheer ice walls with minimal equipment. Yet Haldrik showed a peculiar adaptation to these trials. Where others struggled against the cold, he seemed to embrace it, developing a fighting style that used the freezing conditions to his advantage.

In one memorable training session, Thokk had paired the initiates for combat practice on a narrow ice bridge. While other young warriors tried to overpower their opponents with brute force, Haldrik would shift his weight subtly, letting his opponents' own momentum carry them into the freezing winds, using the cold itself as a weapon. Thokk noted with approval how the young warrior had learned to "dance with winter."

THE MYSTERIES OF THE DEEP  
But as the sun set each day, while other warriors retired to celebrate their bruises with strong drinks and boasting, Haldrik descended into the deeper chambers with Morgatha Frostweaver. The ancient priestess taught him the secret ways of preserving sacred flesh, the hidden meanings in the patterns of frost that formed on cave walls, and the interpretations of Quajath's whispers in the wind.  
Under her tutelage, Haldrik learned that true power lay not just in the strength of arms, but in understanding the deep mysteries their god had left frozen in time. Morgatha showed him how to read the stories written in layers of ancient ice, how to interpret the dreams that came after tasting preserved flesh, and how to hear the subtle differences between ordinary cave winds and divine whispers.

TRIALS OF BLOOD AND ICE  
His dual training came to a head during his fifteenth year, at the Warrior's Trials. While other initiates faced traditional combat challenges, the Wormtongue Council decreed that Haldrik would face a unique test. He was sealed in the Whispering Depths \- a chamber normally forbidden to all but the highest priests \- with only a ceremonial dagger and a single piece of preserved flesh.

For six days, no sound emerged from the sacred chamber. The tribe began preparations for a death ceremony, as none had ever survived so long in those depths. But on the seventh day, Haldrik emerged, his body covered in self-inscribed ritual scars that matched ancient patterns even the Council had forgotten. In his hands, he carried a weapon that would become his signature \- an ice-crystal spear that seemed to hum with its own inner resonance.

THE RISE OF A SEER-WARRIOR  
The years following Haldrik's trial marked a unique position within the Bloodmaw Clan. Neither pure warrior nor traditional priest, he carved out his own role. While other warriors boasted of their kills, Haldrik spoke of visions seen in the patterns of frozen blood on snow. Where traditional priests spent their days in preservation chambers, he led hunting parties that seemed guided by more than mere tracking skills.

His hunting methods unnerved even veteran warriors. He would stand motionless for hours, listening to wind patterns that only he could interpret, then lead the party unerringly to their prey. Several times, he halted hunts moments before unseen ice sheets would have collapsed beneath them. The younger warriors began calling him "Ice-Touched" \- a name that carried equal parts respect and wariness.

OMENS OF CHANGE  
During his twentieth winter, the patterns began changing. The preserved flesh spoke to him of movements in the deep ice, of ancient things stirring beneath the frozen surface. His dreams filled with visions of vast chambers beneath the glaciers, holding secrets that made the sacred flesh in their caves seem like mere scraps.  
The Wormtongue Council listened to his visions with growing concern. Some, like Varrax the Ancient, saw them as signs of divine favor. Others, particularly Korgath Bloodspeaker, warned that such dreams might be tests \- or worse, attempts to lure their warriors away from traditional hunting grounds.

THE CALLING  
The decision was made during the last Frost Feast. As the tribe gathered for the sacred consumption rituals, Haldrik experienced his most powerful vision yet. Before the entire clan, he spoke of a vast chamber of ancient flesh, preserved in ice older than the tribe itself. His words carried such power that the ceremonial flames froze in their braziers, and frost patterns formed on the walls matching his description of the hidden place.

The Council debated for three days. Finally, it was decreed: Haldrik would lead a small party north, beyond their traditional hunting grounds, to seek this ancient cache. Some saw it as a divine mission, others as a convenient way to remove an uncomfortable presence from the tribe's power structure. Either way, as the aurora danced overhead, Haldrik led his chosen warriors into the unknown reaches of the frozen waste.

THE RAID  
Following his visions north, Haldrik led his party to a small fishing settlement on the coast. Under cover of darkness, they overwhelmed the skeleton crew of a fishing vessel. The raid was silent, efficient \- the way of the Bloodmaw. As dawn broke, they were already far from shore, following the signs that Haldrik alone could read in the waves.  
Three days later, they spotted their true prey \- a large merchant vessel heading north, heavy with supplies and men seeking fortune in Eiselcross. The small fishing boat allowed them to approach without raising alarm. The merchants likely thought them fellow fortune-seekers or perhaps survivors needing aid.

THE BATTLE  
They struck at dusk, when the watch was changing. The initial assault went as planned \- Bloodmaw warriors scaling the hull while Yurax steered their smaller vessel alongside. But among the merchant crew was a man unlike the others. He fought with strange weapons that sparked and thundered, his movements precise and deadly despite his apparent fear.  
Chaos erupted across the deck. The fishing boat, still tied alongside, caught fire from knocked-over lanterns. Warriors and merchants died in pairs, locked in desperate combat. Kestra fell first, her throat caught by a lucky knife thrust. Yurax and two others went down with the burning fishing boat, the weight of their ice-forged armor dragging them into the depths.  
In the final moments, only Haldrik and the strange gunslinger remained standing \- each wounded, each having witnessed their companions fall. They faced each other across a deck slick with blood and ice, neither strong enough to ensure victory, both knowing death waited in the frozen waters below.

STALEMATE  
The choice was made for them as the ship groaned beneath their feet. The battle had taken its toll \- damaged rigging, torn sails, and no one left alive who knew how to repair them. The stranger spoke first, introducing himself as Samuel Winters. His words were foreign, but his meaning clear \- fight and die, or cooperate and maybe live.

Much of the ship's supplies had burned in the battle. What remained they divided equally, each claiming one end of the vessel. Days passed in tense silence, broken only by necessary communication about weather or warnings of ice. Neither man slept deeply, always keeping one hand on their weapons.

THE HUNGER  
As supplies dwindled, the situation grew dire. The bodies of fallen crew and warriors remained scattered across the deck, preserved by the cold. Haldrik knew the ritual prayers for sacred consumption, but recognized such actions would likely spark a final, fatal confrontation with Winters.

Instead, they rationed what little remained, each growing weaker as the ship drifted aimlessly through the frigid waters. Sometimes Haldrik would catch Winters staring at him with an unreadable expression, and wondered if the stranger was calculating the same grim equations about survival.

Now they drift, two enemies turned reluctant survivors, neither willing to trust nor able to survive alone. The last of their food gone, both men stand at the edge of decisions that would horrify them in better times. The frozen corpses remain untouched, but each passing hour of hunger makes them harder to ignore.

THE DRIFTING DAYS  
The merchant vessel \- "The Winter's Bounty" according to its weather-worn nameplate \- became both sanctuary and prison. The once-spacious deck now felt like a cage, divided by an invisible line neither man crossed. Haldrik claimed the bow, where the winds carried away the smell of the corpses he couldn't honor with proper rites. Winters kept to the stern, his strange weapons always within reach, spending hours staring at a silver locket he thought no one could see him holding.  
UNSPOKEN RULES  
Their survival pact developed its own rigid code:

\- Never approach while the other sleeps  
\- No moving about the ship without announcing intentions  
\- Equal divisions of any supplies found  
\- Maintain distance during necessary deck work  
\- Watch shifts divided equally  
\- No touching the other's weapons or possessions

DESPERATE MEASURES  
When they found the ship's water supply tainted by salt, both men worked together to rig a system of catching ice and snow. The cooperation lasted only as long as necessary \- each returning to their assigned spaces the moment the task was done. Even in their weakened state, neither dropped their guard.  
The food ran out faster than either expected. They discovered rats had infiltrated much of the remaining stores, spoiling what little was left. Their last meal \- a handful of salt-tough beef and moldy hardtack \- they divided with the precision of suspicious merchants, each watching the other's portion with hollow eyes.

DESCENT  
As hunger took hold, their worlds began to shrink. Haldrik found himself spending hours staring at the frozen bodies, reciting preservation rites under his breath. The teachings of his people offered spiritual justification for survival consumption, yet something held him back \- perhaps the way Winters seemed to watch him during these moments, hand never far from his weapon.  
Winters developed a habit of talking to himself, or perhaps to the locket he carried. Sometimes in common tongue, sometimes in languages Haldrik didn't recognize. The hobgoblin caught fragments about family, about mistakes, about desperate searches for something important.

THE EDGE  
Now they've reached the precipice where civilization's thin veneer begins to crack. Both men have the same thoughts, neither willing to speak them aloud. The corpses remain preserved by the merciless cold, an option becoming harder to ignore. Each man watches the other for signs of breaking, for the moment when hunger overwhelms honor.

In the distance, strange lights dance on the horizon. Neither man trusts their eyes anymore \- too many false hopes have appeared and vanished. They drift, two enemies bound by circumstance, each wondering if they'll survive long enough to become enemies again.

Backstory  
Family Name: Frostwhisper  
Parents: Aelindra (mother) and Thaelar (father) Frostwhisper Theon brother

Lilith and Theon Frostwhisper were born in Palebank Village, a small Uthodurnian fishing outpost nestled against the Frigid Depths, just north of the Flotket Alps. Their parents, Aelindra and Thaelar, were skilled ice fishers and trappers, providing essential food supplies for both their village and the city of Uthodurn.

From an early age, the twins exhibited starkly different personalities. Lilith was always the daring one, her eyes constantly drawn to the horizon and the mysteries beyond their small village. Theon, on the other hand, was more reserved, finding comfort in the familiar and safety in routine.

Growing up in the harsh, wintry climate of Palebank Village, the twins learned early on about the importance of community and survival. Their days were filled with helping their parents prepare fishing nets, learning to identify edible plants that could survive in the tundra, and assisting in preserving the catches for trade and storage.

Despite the challenging environment, Lilith and Theon found joy in their childhood. They would often sneak away to play in the Crystalsands Tundra, marveling at how the snow glittered like diamonds in the sunlight. They made up games involving the various ships that docked in the village, imagining grand adventures to distant lands.

As they grew older, the twins began to notice the frequent expeditions to Eiselcross. They would watch with wide eyes as members of the Cobalt Soul, Cerberus Assembly, and other factions would pass through their village, bringing tales of ancient ruins and mysterious artifacts. These stories sparked a sense of wanderlust in Lilith, while Theon became more interested in the techniques and disciplines of the visitors, particularly the monks.

A pivotal moment in their childhood occurred when they were eight years old. Lilith, fascinated by tales of ancient ruins in Eiselcross, convinced Theon to join her on a secret "expedition" north of the village. Despite his reservations, Theon couldn't let his sister go alone.

As they ventured further than ever before, the weather suddenly turned. A blizzard descended, quickly obscuring their path home. Lilith, excited by the adventure, wanted to press on, certain they'd find shelter in a cave or ruins like in the stories. Theon, however, recognized the danger.

"Lilith, we have to go back," he insisted, grabbing her arm. "This isn't a game. We could freeze out here\!"

But Lilith shook him off. "We've come too far to turn back now\! Don't you want to discover something amazing?"

As the storm worsened, Theon realized arguments wouldn't work. Instead, he closed his eyes, remembering the meditation techniques he'd learned from watching a visiting monk. He focused, sensing the wind's direction and the faint sounds of the village bells carried on the gale.

"Lilith," he said calmly, opening his eyes. "I know you want to explore, but our real adventure is surviving this. Trust me, I can get us home."

Seeing her brother's determination, Lilith finally relented. Theon led them back, using his newfound focus to navigate through the whiteout conditions. When they finally stumbled back into the village, half-frozen but alive, their relief was palpable.

This incident became a turning point in their relationship. Lilith realized that her brother's caution wasn't boring \- it was a different kind of strength. Theon, in turn, recognized that his sister's adventurous spirit, while sometimes reckless, could inspire him to push beyond his comfort zone.

They began to balance each other out. Lilith would come up with wild plans to explore the tundra or sneak onto ships bound for Eiselcross, and Theon would temper these ideas, finding safer ways to satisfy her curiosity. They created a secret hideout in a small sea cave near the village, where Lilith would share her dreams of adventure, and Theon would teach her the meditation techniques that had saved them in the blizzard.

Their hideout became a sanctuary, filled with trinkets from their small adventures \- interesting rocks, pieces of driftwood, and even a map of Eiselcross that Lilith had painstakingly copied from a book in the village hall. They made a pact there, swearing to always look out for each other, no matter where life took them.

As their tenth nameday approached, tensions began to rise between the twins. Lilith spoke more and more about grand adventures, while Theon felt torn between his love for his sister and his growing interest in the disciplined life of the monks who occasionally visited.

On the day of their tenth nameday celebration, a group from the Cobalt Soul arrived in Palebank Village, preparing for an expedition to Eiselcross. Among them was a monk named Elowen Greymantle, who took notice of young Theon's focus and maturity.

Seeing an opportunity to learn more about the techniques that had saved their lives, Theon approached Elowen and asked to be taken as an apprentice. After a long discussion with the Frostwhisper family, Elowen agreed to take Theon under her wing, promising to train him in the ways of the Cobalt Soul.

The decision was met with mixed emotions. Aelindra and Thaelar were proud but worried for their son's safety. Lilith, however, felt betrayed and angry. As Theon prepared to leave, Lilith confronted him:

"You always said it was too dangerous out there\! Was that all a lie? Or are you just leaving me behind?" she demanded, tears in her eyes.

Theon, torn between his love for his family and his desire to learn, tried to explain. "I'm doing this for us, Lilith. I'll learn things that can keep us both safe when we have real adventures together. I promise I'll come back and tell you everything."

But Lilith, feeling abandoned and suffocated by the suddenly too-small village, turned away from her brother. As Theon boarded the ship with Elowen, Lilith watched from the cliffs, a mix of anger, sadness, and envy swirling in her heart. She vowed to find her own path, one that would prove she didn't need her brother or anyone else to protect her.

As the ship disappeared into the horizon, Lilith stood alone, the icy wind whipping around her. She felt a newfound determination. If Theon wouldn't be there to hold her back, she'd show him just how far she could go on her own. Unbeknownst to her, this fierce resolve would soon attract the attention of darker forces, setting her on the path to becoming a warlock.

Here's the backstory for Lilith from age 10 to the eve of her brother's return:

After Theon's departure, Lilith threw herself into village life with a fierce determination. She helped the Glassblades patrol the village outskirts, fought off wild beasts, and took on extra watch shifts. Her bravery and assertiveness earned her respect, but she remained unsatisfied, constantly hearing tales of Theon's achievements at the Cobalt Soul.

Though she feigned disinterest, each tale stoked the embers of jealousy in her heart. She stubbornly refused to write to him, her anger at his departure still raw.

One week before the Ironbloom Festival, news arrived that Theon would be returning to Palebank Village. Lilith, feeling a mixture of anticipation and dread, retreated to the sea cave she once shared with her brother. As she sat brooding, strange whispers echoed through the cavern. Suddenly, the ground gave way beneath her.

Lilith found herself in a vast, winding network of underground passages. Drawn by the mysterious whispers, she navigated the treacherous caverns until she came upon a chamber where a necklace floated above a stone pillar. A voice, smooth as silk but with an underlying current of danger, spoke to her.

"Free me, child of ambition, and I shall grant you power beyond your wildest dreams. You'll never again stand in anyone's shadow."

Blinded by her desire to prove herself and her adventurous spirit, Lilith reached for the necklace. As her fingers closed around it, she felt a surge of heat course through her body. Unbeknownst to her, she had just made a pact with an Efreeti, a powerful fire genie.

The Efreeti, now bound to the necklace and to Lilith, explained that it needed to rest to gather strength. It promised her more power as she grew stronger, failing to mention its true intention to eventually possess her body and consume her soul.

Lilith emerged from the cave transformed. She no longer felt the biting cold of Palebank, and small flames danced at her fingertips. Exhilarated by her new abilities, she spent the next few days secretly practicing her magic, creating small fires and reveling in her newfound warmth.

As night fell on the eve of Theon's return, Lilith stood on the cliffs overlooking the Frigid Depths. The necklace pulsed warmly against her skin as she conjured a small flame in her palm. A mix of excitement and apprehension filled her as she thought about reuniting with her brother. She was no longer the girl he had left behind \- she now had secrets and power of her own. 

Tomorrow would bring their reunion and the start of the Ironbloom Festival. But for now, Lilith allowed herself a small, satisfied smile as she extinguished the flame and turned back towards the village, unaware of the true nature of the pact she had made and the dangers that lay ahead.

Her father, Thorne Wildflame, was a wood elf ranger who fell in love with her mother, Ember, a crimson-skinned tiefling herbalist. Their union resulted in Willow's unique appearance: a lithe figure with vibrant blue skin, small horns, and eyes that seemed to hold the vastness of the night sky.

Growing up, Willow was always fascinated by nature, but initially in the same way as her peers. She communed with trees, spoke to animals, and learned the ways of the forest. It wasn't until she met her mentor, Celeste Moonglow, that her perspective began to shift. Celeste, an elderly female druid, saw great potential in the young tiefling and introduced her to a broader view of nature.

Willow's closest friend in the village was Briar Oakenheart, a human druid who specialized in plant magic. The two were inseparable, often exploring the forests together and sharing their discoveries. Willow and Aspen often found themselves competing in druidic rituals within the village, each trying to prove the superiority of their chosen path. As their rivalry intensified, so did Willow's determination to prove that cosmic nature was just as valid as earthly nature.

When Willow regained consciousness, she found herself alone in the icy tundra of the Greying Wildlands, far from home. Cold, confused, and wracked with guilt, she vowed to find her mentor and rival before ever returning to face the consequences of her actions in the village.

Klaus The Hollow

KLAUS  
"The Hollow"  
Tarandus Ranger (Grim Harbinger) | Level 6 | Age 35  
\~ \~ \~  
In the heart of the Feywild, where the silver light of eternal twilight filters through canopies of impossible colors, the Herd of the Silver Trail has wandered for millennia. They are tarandus \- tall, antlered folk devoted to the art of exploration and mapmaking. Their map-halls contain charts of places that no longer exist, paths through dreams, and routes between worlds. Every member of the herd carries within them a spirit animal, a bond forged at the threshold of adulthood that guides them through the wild places of existence.  
Every member, except one.  
Klaus was born to Soleir, a quiet cartographer whose maps were said to show not just where things were, but where they were meant to be, and Neiira, a hunter whose tracking skills were legendary even among a people known for finding their way. They raised their son with patience and love, teaching him the ways of the hunt, the language of hoofprints in snow, the whisper of wind through ancient trees. Klaus learned well. His skills were sharp. His instincts were true.  
But when the time came for the ritual \- when every young tarandus reaches into the spirit realm and calls forth the animal that will walk beside them forever \- Klaus reached out and found nothing. Only silence. Only emptiness.  
He tried again the following year. And the year after that. Each time, the elders watched with diminishing hope. Each time, his peers whispered louder. Each time, the silence grew heavier.  
They called him Klaus the Hollow.  
It was not just the humiliation that ate at him \- though that was bitter enough. It was the feeling of uselessness. Among a people where every soul walked with a spirit companion, he was incomplete. His hunting skills meant nothing. His sharp eyes and steady hands meant nothing. Without a spirit animal, he could never truly contribute. He would always be lesser than.  
Only one person never looked at him with pity or contempt. Eira, his childhood friend, insisted year after year that he simply had not found the right spirit yet. That it was out there, waiting. That he was not broken, just different. Klaus wanted to believe her. Most days, he could not.  
\~ \~ \~  
The tarandus knew of many portals leading from the Feywild to other planes. Most were far from the villages, guarded not by the herd but by fey creatures who had taken up the duty centuries ago. Few tarandus ever used them \- why leave a realm of wonder for the mundane worlds beyond?  
But Klaus began to wonder. If no spirit in the Feywild would answer his call, perhaps one existed elsewhere. Perhaps his spirit animal was not of this realm at all.  
He found his way to one such portal, guarded by a satyr named Thornwick. Unlike most fey, Thornwick had mellowed with the long centuries \- more philosophical than mischievous, more interested in conversation than tricks. He had watched that portal for so long that the watching had become a kind of meditation.  
Klaus visited him often. They talked. The satyr listened to the young tarandus speak of failure and shame, of desperate ideas and impossible hopes. Thornwick saw something familiar in Klaus \- the look of someone who did not quite fit the mold they were born into.  
When Klaus finally asked to pass through, Thornwick did not hesitate. He stepped aside \- not out of pity, but respect.  
At twenty-seven years old, Klaus the Hollow stepped out of the Feywild and into the unknown.  
\~ \~ \~  
The portal deposited him in the Lotusden Greenwood, an ancient forest in Wildemount that still carried faint echoes of fey magic. The trees were different here \- more solid, less likely to whisper secrets \- but something in the air felt almost familiar.  
He wandered for days before he found Mosshollow.  
It was a small village, tucked away in the deepest parts of the Greenwood, founded by those who had reason to hide. The elders spoke in hushed tones of poachers who stalked Exandria \- traffickers who hunted exotic beings, who kidnapped and broke and sold rare creatures at underground auctions. They called themselves the Galleria, and their cruelty was legendary among demihuman communities.  
Mosshollow was built on secrecy. Its people did not travel. They stayed together, protected each other, and prayed they would never be found.  
The village was home to beast-folk of all kinds \- a gruff minotaur chief named Varek, who tested Klaus's hunting skills personally before allowing him to stay. A bugbear named Bram, quiet and dependable, who became Klaus's closest friend and hunting partner. A tabaxi named Yenna, fast-talking and full of gossip, who could always make Klaus laugh. Old Marsh, a firbolg herbalist who gave Klaus tea and advice without ever asking about his past. Siora, an owlin fletcher who made Klaus his first Exandrian bow. Young Quillan, a grung who followed Klaus everywhere, asking endless questions about the Feywild.  
Klaus was the only deer-like humanoid they had ever seen. But he was not the strangest thing in Mosshollow, and that was a comfort.  
More importantly \- no one cared about his lack of a spirit animal. They cared that he could hunt. That he could track. That he showed up when needed and did his share. For the first time in his life, Klaus was valued for what he could do, not judged for what he lacked.  
He still attempted the ritual sometimes, alone in the forest, reaching out for something that never answered. It still failed. But it mattered less here. He had found a place where he belonged.  
He built a home. He made friends. He allowed himself, cautiously, to hope.  
\~ \~ \~  
Klaus never fully severed his ties to the Feywild. He returned periodically through the portal to visit his parents \- Soleir with his quiet faith that Klaus would find his way, Neiira with her fierce protectiveness against anyone who dared mock her son.  
These visits were bittersweet. The herd still whispered. Elder Solwyn, ancient leader of the Silver Trail, treated Klaus with a kind of sad dignity \- seeing his failure as a spiritual wound rather than a character flaw, but unable to shield him from the judgment of others.  
Still, Klaus came and went. The portal remained open. Thornwick always let him pass with a knowing nod.  
What Klaus did not know \- what he could not have known \- was that he was being watched.  
Figures in dark cloaks. Shadow where their faces should be. They had been following a group called the Galleria for reasons unknown, observing the poachers' movements across Wildemount. They witnessed Klaus \- a creature clearly not of this world \- and they watched him step through a doorway into another realm entirely.  
The Faceless Ones were patient. They were curious. And now they knew of a path to somewhere new.  
\~ \~ \~  
The Faceless Ones entered the Feywild.  
What they did there, Klaus would never fully understand. They performed rituals \- wrong, unnatural things that made reality itself recoil. The devastation they left behind was not destruction in any ordinary sense.  
A chunk of the Feywild was simply gone. Missing. As if something had bitten into the plane itself and swallowed a piece whole. The village of the Herd of the Silver Trail, the surrounding forest, the map-halls with their impossible charts \- all of it vanished, leaving behind a wound in existence. What remained at the edges was cold and dark and wrong, a scar that hurt to look at.  
Some of the herd were found dead. Others had simply vanished without trace \- no bodies, no blood, no explanation. Among the missing were Soleir and Neiira.  
One tarandus escaped the devastation and tracked Klaus down in Wildemount.  
Eira.  
\~ \~ \~  
She found him in Mosshollow, exhausted from the journey, eyes haunted by what she had seen. She told him everything \- the destruction, the dead, the missing. His parents among them.  
Klaus did not hesitate. He went with her immediately, back through the portal, back to the Feywild.  
Nothing could have prepared him for what he found.  
The void where his home had been. The wrongness of the air. The cold, dark emptiness that remained where the Silver Trail had wandered for millennia. He stood at the edge of the wound and felt something break inside him.  
And beneath the grief, a terrible suspicion began to form. He had crossed between worlds. He had been careless. He had been followed.  
This was his fault.  
He did not speak this fear aloud. He could not. But it settled into his bones like poison.  
Klaus told Eira they needed help \- his hunter friends in Mosshollow had skills and resources. They could investigate, track down answers, find the missing. She agreed. They returned to Wildemount.  
And found Mosshollow in ruins.  
\~ \~ \~  
Not the Faceless Ones this time. The Galleria. The poachers the elders had always feared, the traffickers who hunted exotic beings for their underground auctions. They had finally found the hidden village.  
Mosshollow's one defense had been secrecy, and secrecy had failed.  
Yenna was dead. The fast-talking tabaxi who always made Klaus laugh \- gone. He found evidence of her end and could not look away, could not stop seeing it.  
Bram was missing. His closest friend, his hunting partner, the quiet bugbear who had accepted him without question \- taken. Likely in a cage somewhere, waiting to be sold.  
The others were scattered. Some escaped into the Greenwood. Some were taken. Some were killed. Chief Varek, Old Marsh, Siora, young Quillan \- their fates unknown.  
Two homes. Two peoples. Both devastated.  
One destroyed by the Faceless Ones who had followed Klaus to the Feywild. One raided by the Galleria while Klaus was away.  
Coincidence? Fate? Curse?  
Klaus did not know. But he knew one thing with terrible certainty: he had not been there to protect either. Everything he touched was destroyed. Everyone who sheltered him suffered for it.  
Klaus the Hollow. Not just spiritless \- but poison.  
\~ \~ \~  
A period of grief passed. Klaus wanted blood \- wanted to hunt down the Galleria and make them pay for what they had done. But Eira urged focus. Start at the source, she said. Return to the Feywild. Seek out other tribes. Find allies. Find answers. Then hunt.  
Klaus agreed. What else could he do?  
They made their way back to the portal. But something was wrong.  
The portal flickered. Distorted. The light that usually shimmered with the colors of the Feywild now churned with something darker, something unstable.  
Eira stepped through first.  
Through the shimmer, Klaus glimpsed where she arrived \- not the silver twilight of the Feywild, but a dark place. An unfamiliar sky. Three moons hanging above a landscape he had never seen.  
This was not the Feywild.  
Klaus did not hesitate. He ran through after her.  
He arrived in the same plane \- the same three moons above, the same dark and alien landscape stretching in every direction. But Eira was nowhere to be seen. The portal had deposited them in the same world but different locations, separated by unknown distance.  
The portal snapped shut behind him.  
He was trapped. Alone. In an unknown realm. Separated from the one person who had come to him for help.  
He had failed her too.  
\~ \~ \~  
In this dark plane, something began hunting Klaus.  
A creature of shadow \- wolf-like, savage, made of darkness itself. It emerged from the shadows without warning, slashing with claws that left wounds cold as winter, then vanishing before Klaus could respond. Night after night it came. Klaus was wounded. Exhausted. Bleeding. Hunted like an animal.  
For days he ran. For days he survived by instinct and desperation, staying one step ahead of a predator that seemed to know his every move.  
And then something shifted.  
It happened in a moment of utter exhaustion, when Klaus had nothing left \- no strength, no hope, no plan. The shadow wolf was close. He could feel it circling. And somewhere in the depths of his despair, a cold clarity took hold.  
He would not die as prey.  
If this was his end, he would meet it as a hunter.  
Klaus stopped running. He turned. He began to track.  
And that was when he felt it.  
A resonance. A pull. Something he had never experienced in his entire life, through all those failed rituals, all those silent years of reaching out and finding nothing.  
Not understanding. Not kinship. But instinct \- a bone-deep, primal need to hunt this creature. To find it. To face it.  
Revenge, yes. The beast had wounded him, terrorized him. But this was something more. Something deeper.  
For the first time in thirty-five years, Klaus the Hollow wondered if he was not hollow at all. If what he had been searching for all his life had simply been waiting \- in a dark place, under three moons, wearing the shape of shadow and hunger.  
\~ \~ \~  
For two weeks, Klaus tracked the shadow wolf across the strange land.  
Hunter and hunted, the roles blurring with each passing day. The creature was clever \- it knew this place, knew the shadows, knew how to disappear. But Klaus was relentless. He read the signs no one else could read. He followed trails that existed more in feeling than in fact. The resonance pulled him forward like a thread tied to his heart.  
The trail led him, finally, to a camp in the night.  
Other travelers. Other souls lost in this dark realm, or perhaps natives to it \- Klaus did not know. What he knew was that the shadow wolf was here. Close. Circling.  
He watched from the darkness as someone took the night watch. Watched as the shadows behind them began to move.  
The beast attacked.  
And Klaus, hunter on its heels, emerged from the darkness to meet it.

It happened in a moment of utter exhaustion, when Klaus had nothing left \- no strength, no hope, no plan. The shadow wolf was close. He could feel it circling. And somewhere in the depths of his despair, a cold clarity took hold.  
He would not die as prey.  
If this was his end, he would meet it as a hunter.  
Klaus stopped running. He turned. He began to track.  
And that was when he felt it.  
A resonance. A pull. Something he had never experienced in his entire life, through all those failed rituals, all those silent years of reaching out and finding nothing.  
Not understanding. Not kinship. But instinct \- a bone-deep, primal need to hunt this creature. To find it. To face it.  
Revenge, yes. The beast had wounded him, terrorized him. But this was something more. Something deeper.  
For the first time in thirty-five years, Klaus the Hollow wondered if he was not hollow at all. If what he had been searching for all his life had simply been waiting \- in a dark place, under three moons, wearing the shape of shadow and hunger.  
\~ \~ \~  
For two weeks, Klaus tracked the shadow wolf across the strange land.  
Hunter and hunted, the roles blurring with each passing day. The creature was clever \- it knew this place, knew the shadows, knew how to disappear. But Klaus was relentless. He read the signs no one else could read. He followed trails that existed more in feeling than in fact. The resonance pulled him forward like a thread tied to his heart.  
The trail led him, finally, to a camp in the night.  
Other travelers. Other souls lost in this dark realm, or perhaps natives to it \- Klaus did not know. What he knew was that the shadow wolf was here. Close. Circling.  
He watched from the darkness as someone took the night watch. Watched as the shadows behind them began to move.  
The beast attacked.  
And Klaus, hunter on its heels, emerged from the darkness to meet it.

A CHARACTER HISTORY  
The Hunt That Came For Him  
The Tale of Arlan

Shifter  ·  Blood Hunter  
They took a man who tracked monsters.They gave him back as one.

I.   The Boy On The Long Road  
Before any of the rest of it, there was a wagon road in good country, and a boy who thought the whole world was that road.  
Arlan was born to a family of travelling tradesfolk \- tinkers and trackers both, a small clan of people who never owned a door because they never needed one. His mother, Saela, was the tracker of the family. She could read a hillside the way other people read a face, and from the time Arlan could walk she taught him that the wild was not a danger to be feared but a language to be learned: the grammar of bent grass, the punctuation of a broken twig, the long slow sentences a hunted thing writes behind it without ever meaning to. His father, Corwen, was the gentler half of the pair, a mender of pots and harness and broken things, slow to anger and quick to laugh, forever telling the same six stories as though no one had heard them before. They were not rich. They were not safe, exactly, not the way settled folk are safe. But they were yoked together, and they were warm, and a child raised in a warm wagon does not imagine there is any other way to be raised.  
He had a sister, younger by four years. Rama was a fierce, sharp-eyed thing who followed him everywhere and bit anyone who frightened him before he could think to be frightened himself. The two of them were inseparable in the particular way of children who have no fixed neighbours and so must be each other's whole world. Arlan taught her to whistle and to set a snare. She taught him, the way little sisters do, exactly how brave he was not. He loved her past all sense.  
And there was Elina. Elina Marrowen was a hearth-keeper's daughter from a hill town the family wintered in three years running, and what began as two young people circling each other shyly across a market square became, slowly and certainly, the thing Arlan's quiet future was built around. By the time he was a grown man it was understood between them. That last year he had begun to speak of stopping \- of trading the long road for one door, one hearth, one town with Elina's window in it. He had even started to want it, and for a man raised to believe that wanting things was dangerous, that was no small surrender.  
He had work by then, and he was good at it. The travelling life and his mother's teaching had made Arlan a hunter \- not of game, but of the things that hunt. When a village lost a child to the deep wood, or woke to slaughtered flocks and tracks that were no wolf's tracks, they sent for men like Arlan: patient, hard, willing to walk into the dark after a thing and not come back out until it was finished. He had earned a small name in a small circle of frightened towns, and it was enough. He was an ordinary man \- a hunter, a son, a brother, a husband-to-be \- and the shape of his days suited him, and he asked for nothing more than to keep them.  
II.   The Night The Road Ended  
It came on an ordinary night, which is the only kind of night such things ever come on.  
The family had made camp off the road in country Arlan knew. There was the fire, and Rama complaining about the cold, and his father halfway through the third of the six stories. Then his mother went still \- Saela, who read hillsides, going still the way a deer goes still \- and said, in a voice Arlan had never once heard her use, that the night had gone wrong, that there were no insects, that something had reached in and pinched the world quiet.  
Then they were simply there. Tall shapes in dark cloaks, more of them than the firelight would give back, and where each face should have been there was nothing \- not a mask, not a shadowed hood, but an absence, a smear, a place where a face had been rubbed out of the world.  
Arlan fought. He was strong, and fast, and the wild had taught him to be vicious when being vicious was all that was left \- and it did not matter. The faceless ones did not hurry and did not speak. They took the family apart the way a man takes apart a fence that happens to be in his way: without anger, with tools. His father shouted his name. He reached for Rama's hand and closed on nothing. There was iron at his wrists, and the cold weightless lift of being carried by something that felt no strain at all in the carrying of him.  
The last thing the world gave him was a sound like cloth tearing very slowly, and a light that was not light, and then the ground he had walked his entire life was not beneath him. He did not see what became of Saela, of Corwen, of Rama. He did not see whether the faceless ones crossed the half-day of road to Elina's hill town. He did not see anything at all. He was simply taken, and the camp and the fire and the six stories were gone behind him, and he did not know if a single soul he loved was alive or dead.  
III.   The Months In The Cage  
He woke in a cage, and he did not leave it for a long, long time.  
It was a cold place that smelled of metal and chemical and old fear, lit by an even, sourceless grey light that never brightened and never dimmed \- a light built, he came to understand, for the specific purpose of taking time away from a person. With no day and no night to hang the hours on, the months ran together. They became a single grey horror that he had fallen into and could not climb back out of.  
These captors were not the faceless ones. These had faces, and that was worse, because a face means a person, and a person was choosing to do this. They wore aprons stained dark and gloves they never took off, and they carried a sharp chemical reek that got into the back of his throat and stayed there. They were not cruel the way an angry man is cruel. They were cruel the way a careful man is careful, and that was the thing that broke something in him \- not rage, but their patience.  
They did not question him and they did not punish him. They used him as material. They opened him and studied him and closed him again. They made him swallow draughts that set something crawling under his skin like a living thing. They tested how much of him could be taken away before he stopped working, and then gave back just enough to keep him working, and then took again. He learned that the worst pain in the world is not the sharp kind; it is the patient kind, the kind with no anger anywhere in it, the kind that only intends to continue. Somewhere in that grey stretch the ordinary man who had meant to build a hearth beside Elina Marrowen came quietly apart, and what was left in the cage kept his memories but not his peace.  
And the thing they were making of him began to take. Arlan had been born an ordinary man with one ordinary shape, and he did not stay one. He woke from sleeps he had not agreed to with his body subtly rearranged \- a joint that bent a new way, skin gone strange across the back of one hand, a face in the dark glass of the cage that was almost his and not quite. The change rooted itself all the way down. His shape stopped being a fixed thing he simply lived inside and became something restless beneath his own thoughts, remaking itself on its own animal instinct, settling each time he slept into some new readiness he had not chosen. They had reached into whatever a man is made of and pried it loose from its mooring. He had no word for what he was becoming. He only knew that his own body had stopped entirely belonging to him.  
And then one day a man in stained gloves stood at his bars a long while, watching him the way a man watches a blade while deciding whether it is worth the keeping. He nodded once, to someone Arlan could not see. Hands took hold of the cage. Arlan's time as a thing to be studied was over, and his time as a thing to be used had begun.  
IV.   The Brand And The House  
They moved him to a finer place, and a finer place, in the hands of such people, only means a more valuable use.  
This was a hall, not a cage \- the seat of the gloved, patient people themselves, the ones who had remade him. He came to think of them only as the House, because that was the word their own servants used, hushed and a little proud, as though belonging to it were an honour and not a sentence. The House dealt in flesh: in the changing of it, the improving of it, in making bodies do things bodies were never meant to do. Arlan was their proof that it could be done.  
They marked him as theirs. He was held down \- he fought, of course he fought, he had never once in his life not fought \- and a brand was set into him, hot iron biting the House's sigil deep into his skin so that anyone who saw him stripped would know at a glance whose property he was. But the brand was the smallest cruelty of that hall, because the brand was only ownership, and ownership, at least, he could understand.  
They put a second thing into him the same day. He never saw it clearly will \- a cold, hard weight, sealed somewhere beneath his skin, set there with a needle and the same chemical stink as everything else they did to him. They told him nothing about it. He understood only that it was theirs, and that it was in him now, and could not be dug out.  
What came after the brand and the needle was the House's true work, and it was far more terrible than any scar. They had spent the long months of the cage learning the substance of him; in the hall, they finished it. Through rites and draughts and instruments he has no names for, they reached into his very blood and taught it to be a weapon. They taught it to be spent and shaped and burned as fuel, to carry power the way a wick carries flame, so that a strike of his could be paid for in his own life and made terrible by the paying. The cage had unmoored his shape. The hall reached past the shape entirely and altered what ran inside him. They did not ask whether he wanted it. They did not call it a gift. They made him into a thing that could turn its own life into a blade, because such a thing would draw a crowd and bleed beautifully, and that was the whole and entire measure of their interest in him.  
This is the root of the particular madness Arlan carries. The power that keeps him alive is the House's craft, stitched into his blood beside their brand. He cannot lift a hand to defend himself without reaching for the very thing they made of him. They are not an enemy he can walk away from and leave behind. They are in his hands. They are in his veins. They are a thing he now is.  
V.   The Pit  
They had not remade him in order to keep him. They had remade him in order to wager him. The House took its new and valuable property and entered it into the Pit.  
The Pit was an arena \- vast, sunk deep into the earth, ringed all the way up to its high black rim with a crowd that Arlan never once saw clearly and never once stopped hearing. The crowd was the constant of that place: a single enormous animal made of thousands of smaller ones, always roaring, always hungry, never in all his time there fed enough. High above the sand sat the ones the crowd performed for \- tiers of cold, comfortable figures set apart and above, the owners, the Houses, who did not roar, because to them the screaming and the dying on the sand below was not sport at all. It was a game they played against one another. Lives were laid down between them like cards in a wager. Arlan was a card. He never learned the rules of the game; he only ever felt himself being played.  
He could not have said where the Pit was. No sky ever opened over the arena's rim. The grey light there never changed any more than it had in the cage. The air carried the stale, sealed taste of a vast room whose door had never once in its existence been left open. He had been put somewhere built, very deliberately, to have no way out of it. He was there roughly six months \- long enough to stop counting the days and start, instead, simply surviving them.  
His House did not want him merely to win. The crowd did not pay to watch a fighter survive; it paid to watch a fighter become. So Arlan was made, on the sand, to perform the very things that had been done to him in the dark: to bleed himself on purpose, to burn his own life into his blows, to let his unmoored body shift and remake itself in the open while thousands screamed for the spectacle of it. The more the crowd screamed, the more pleased his gloved keepers were; and the more pleased they were, the longer he was allowed to keep breathing. He learned the only law the Pit had been built to teach, and he learned it all the way to the bottom of himself: the crowd's hunger was the only coin that place recognised, and feeding it was the only way to live to see another of its changeless grey days.  
VI.   The Friends He Watched Die  
Below the sand, the fighters were kept together in stone pens, and a strange and terrible thing happens to people held together in a place like that. They become, against all sense and against their own better judgement, a kind of family. Arlan did not want one. He had learned in the cage exactly what caring for a thing could cost a person. He got one anyway.  
There was Drummoch, an old and deeply scarred orc who had survived the Pit longer than anyone else below the sand and had, somewhere along the way, simply stopped fearing it. He took the newly-arrived Arlan in hand and taught him how the place truly worked \- which guards could be read and which could not, how the sand drank blood, how a man paced himself across a fight he could not afford to lose. In a place with no fathers in it, Drummoch was a gruff and steady father-shape, and Arlan held to him the way a drowning man holds to a spar.  
There was Isabel, a young captive, quick and bright and far too gentle for that place, who reminded Arlan so unbearably of his sister Rama that the first time she laughed in the dark of the pens it physically hurt him. He appointed himself her shield without ever once saying the words aloud. Keeping Isabel alive became one of the only things in the whole of the Pit that still felt like a choice of his own making.  
And there was Gin, a wry, joking human who insisted on laughing because laughing was the single thing the Pit had not yet found a way to make him stop doing. He and Arlan traded grim humour back and forth across the black of the pens the way other men in better places trade tobacco, and it kept them both, for a while, recognisably themselves.  
And then the Pit did the thing the Pit was built to do. It set them against one another. The Houses found it amusing \- it sharpened the spectacle, the wagering took on an edge \- to make friends fight friends, and so on certain days a fighter was sent out onto the sand to face a face he knew from the dark of the pens. Arlan was made to do this. He does not speak of the particulars of it. He carries them in the same locked place he carries the brand. And on the days he was not himself on the sand, he was made instead to watch \- made to stand at the pen bars and see people he had bled beside, joked beside, sworn without words to protect, cut down for the pleasure of a screaming crowd and the idle, cold-eyed amusement of the boxes above.  
He saw friends die in front of him. More than once. And each time, something in him that had somehow still been soft went hard, and stayed hard. He stopped grieving the deaths cleanly, the way grief is meant to be done; the grief curdled instead into something with teeth in it. He was no longer a frightened man enduring a nightmare and waiting for it to end. He had become a furnace in the shape of a man, and every death he was forced to witness was one more log thrown onto a fire that had forgotten how to go out. He knew himself to be a victim of the thing, and he knew that none of them \- not Drummoch, not Isabel, not Gin, not the strangers, not himself \- had ever done a single thing to deserve it, and that knowledge did not soften him. It maddened him. It set into him a cold, fixed, patient madness, shaped exactly like a debt that has not been paid: a rage at the faceless ones who had torn him from his family and never once told him why; a rage at the House that had branded him and rebuilt his blood for profit; a rage at the Pit that made him kill his own and then made him watch his own be killed. It is not a hot rage and it is not a loud one. It learned its patience from the cage, from the very people he hates most. It only waits in him, and it fully intends to be paid.  
VII.   The Failed Door  
He tried, once, to escape on his own.  
He had spent his whole life learning how a hunted thing thinks \- where it runs, and when, and why \- and so he turned all of that, at last, on the place that held him. He watched the guards the way he had once watched a tree line. He counted the doors. He waited, and he chose his moment, and he ran. For a few breathing miles of stone corridor and broken light he was not a card and not a property. He was simply a man, loose, moving, and he let himself believe it.  
They did not chase him. They did not need to. Somewhere far behind him, a gloved hand pressed something small, and the cold sealed weight the House had set beneath his skin came awake.  
The pain that came out of it was a pain Arlan had not known a body could hold. It did not strike him from outside, the way a blow does. It bloomed outward from the thing inside him, filled every part of him at once, folded him to the stone and kept him there, and it did not stop because there was no part of him it had not already reached. He could not run from a thing that was running through his own veins. He lay where he had fallen until they came at their leisure and collected him, and he understood, lying there, the full and exact truth of his ownership. They had not merely caught him. They had reached into him from a distance and switched him off, the way a man pinches out a candle, and they had done it without hurrying, because the thing in his flesh meant he had never once been out of their reach at all.  
VIII.   The Night Of Many Doors  
The escape that finally took him out was not earned by any cleverness of Arlan's. It was handed to him by the Pit's own arrogance, the way all cruel and overconfident things are, in the end, careless with themselves.  
It began as a wrongness he felt through the soles of his feet before he heard it \- a deep shudder running up through the stone of the pens. Then a roar that was not the crowd's roar. Then the world above the sand came apart. He never learned the cause; an explosion, vast and white and total, somewhere in the great hidden machinery of the place. The arena's high rim cracked across like a dropped bowl. The endless roar of the crowd curdled all at once into the very different sound of a crowd that has just understood it is going to die. The grey, changeless light failed.  
And in the dark and the falling stone, Arlan felt the cold sealed weight under his skin give a single hard pulse against him, sharp as a struck nerve \- and then go still. The pain he braced for did not come. The thing simply stopped. Whatever the blast had broken, it had broken that too, and the leash inside him went quiet and dead. For the first time since the hall, no part of him belonged to anyone else. He was off it. He was free of it. He had no time to wonder at it, and no wish to.  
Because the doors had bloomed open. Not doors \- the tearing-lights, the same wrong wounds in the air that the faceless ones had once carried him through, opening everywhere at once as the sealed place tore itself apart from the inside. A dozen of them. More than he could count. Each one a ragged hole punched clean through to somewhere else, and Arlan a hunter to the very marrow of him, who knew above every other thing the one law that mattered now:  
when the snare breaks open, you do not stop to ask where the gap leads.  
He had time for one choice and no more. Drummoch and Isabel and Gin were somewhere in the same collapsing dark, and he called their names into the screaming and the falling stone and got nothing back but more screaming and more stone, and the tearing-lights were already guttering shut, one after another after another. So he made the choice. He picked the nearest wound in the world that nothing was standing in front of, and without slowing, without praying, without one full heartbeat of hesitation, he threw himself bodily into it, and left the burning Pit and every face in it behind him in the dark.  
IX.   The Land With No Sun  
The crossing was agony. The tearing-light did not carry him gently the way it had always seemed to carry the faceless ones; it dragged him through itself like a body hauled through a thorn hedge, and for a stretch of time that had no length to it at all he was nowhere, and screaming, and not certain that he still had a shape.  
Then there was ground, and cold, and the sudden astonishing fact of being a single solid thing again. He lay against the earth a long while simply breathing. And when at last he lifted his head, the world he lifted it into stopped him where he lay.  
It was dark \- but not the dark of any night. It was a held, even, permanent gloom, a grey dusk with no sun anywhere in it. The air was cold and still and tasted of ash and of something green and very old. And in the sky above him \- because for the first time in longer than he could measure there was a sky, an open sky \- there hung three moons. Three of them. Pale and patient and wrong, looking down upon the dark land below the way the cold figures in the high boxes had looked down upon the sand. This was not his world, and it was not the Pit. It was somewhere else entirely, a country with no name he could read and no rules he had been given.  
He learned the cruelty of that country quickly. It was a land that hated what he was. He had not walked far across it before he understood that the word it used for an outsider was a slur, that strangers fallen out of the tears were met here not as lost souls to be pitied but as a contamination to be found and cleaned away. He had escaped one country that wanted to own him only to land hard in another that simply wanted him gone. There was a bitter, exhausted laugh somewhere in that, and Arlan laughed it once, alone, in the cold and the dark, and then he kept moving \- because a moving thing is harder to corner than a still one, and because moving was, by then, the only prayer he had left in him.  
And here, beneath all his other wounds, is the soft thing the rage is wrapped around \- the thing he would deny outright if any living soul named it to his face. Arlan had a family once. He had a mother and a father, a sister he taught to whistle, a woman whose window he meant to grow old beside, a warm wagon and a long road and a name that was spoken to him without weight. The faceless ones and the House took every part of that from him and gave back, in its place, a brand and a maddened, burning blood. And so what Arlan truly wants, far down underneath the revenge and the madness, is not in honesty vengeance at all. It is to belong to something again. It is to not be, any longer, a single stray and hunted thing alone in the dark. The revenge is the loud thing in him. The loneliness is the true one.  
X.   The Maze Beneath  
His wandering across that sunless country brought him, in the end, to a wound in the earth.  
It was a collapse \- a place where the ground had given way long ago and folded a whole world down into itself. Below the broken lip of it lay ruins: the bones of something built and grand and impossibly old, halls and stairs and pillars all thrown out of true and half-swallowed by stone, a buried city that had died so long ago that even its death was ancient. And it went on. Passages opened off passages, stairwells dropped into stairwells, archways gave onto archways, until it was no longer a ruin but a maze \- a vast, lightless, endless tangle with no centre Arlan could find and no edge he could reach.  
Hunted things go to ground. It is the oldest wisdom there is, and Arlan had spent a lifetime learning it from the other side. A man with a brand on his skin, in a whole bright realm of people who wanted him gone, finds that the lightless places are the only ones still willing to hold him. So he went down. He climbed past the broken lip and into the close stone and the cold and the blind black quiet, and the surface and its three watching moons closed away above him.  
There was light down there, of a kind. Not fire, and not sun. Here and there across the ruined dark grew shelves and clusters of pale fungus, and a few low platforms thick with the same growth, and the fungus gave off a faint, cold, living glow \- a soft blue-green light with no warmth in it, just enough to turn the nearest stone from black to grey and no further. Arlan went from one patch of that quiet glow to the next, the way a man crossing deep water goes from stone to stone, and between them he moved through a darkness so complete it pressed against his open eyes.  
He does not know how deep the maze runs. He does not know what laid these halls, or what unmade them, or whether anything still lives in the black between the glowing fungus. He knows only that the realm above wanted him gone and the dark below did not seem to want anything from him at all, and that, for a hunted man, is close enough to welcome. So Arlan walks the endless maze beneath the world \- branded, changed, his blood made strange, the leash inside him cold and silent, carrying his rage and his loneliness and his small, stubborn, starving hope down into the glowing dark.  
And somewhere ahead of him in that dark, though he has no way of knowing it, there are other footsteps.

Here his history ends, and the campaign begins.

**Backstory**

FANE BELLADONNA  
a life, told from the inside  
There is a path above Hearthdell that nobody else takes. It leaves the village at the back of the Telder fields, past the last of the fences, and it climbs. It is not a road. It is the kind of way that only exists because two pairs of feet have walked it for twenty years, yours and your mother's, up through the pines and the wet stone and the cold that lives in the shadow of the peak even in high summer. The villagers think the mountain is empty. That is the way she has always wanted it.  
Your mother is called Lune, and she is a healer. That is what Hearthdell knows of her, and it is true, and it is also the smallest possible amount of truth a person could tell. She comes down the mountain with a basket of cloth-wrapped remedies, salves for the loggers' hands and bitter teas for the coughs that go through the homesteads every wet season, and she trades them for grain and wool and the occasional coin, and then she climbs home. She is gentle with everyone and close to no one. You have watched her your whole life keep a careful, smiling distance between herself and every soul in that valley, and you grew up thinking that was simply what she was: a quiet woman, a private woman. It took you years to understand that the distance was not her nature. It was her work. She was holding the world at arm's length on purpose, and she was doing it so that no one would ever look too closely at the two of you.  
Because your mother has more than herbs. You have known this since you were small, the way a child knows a thing without being told it \- by the shape of the silences around it. You have seen a man brought to your door blue-lipped and not breathing, and you have seen your mother send you out of the room, and you have stood in the cold listening to nothing, and then heard the man breathe again. You have seen wounds that should have taken a season close inside a night. She never names it. She has never once, in twenty years, performed what she truly is in front of another person if there was any other way. But when there is no other way, when a life is in her hands and herbs will not be enough, you have felt the air in the house go warm and strange, and you have understood that your mother is keeping someone alive with something that does not come out of a basket.  
And she has always, always told you to hide.  
❖  
You are not like the other children of Hearthdell, and you have known that even longer than you have known about your mother. You can fold a pair of wings out of your own back and draw them in again, the way another person might open and close a hand. When you were very young you thought everyone could do this, and you did it once, in delight, where she could see, and the look on her face taught you faster than any words ever could. You do not do it now. You have not done it in years.  
They do not match, your wings. One of them is pale \- feathered, soft, the colour of frost and the faint cold blue of deep ice, and when the light catches it there is something in it that you have caught your mother looking at the way a person looks at a memory. The other is not like that. The other is dark and scaled and the feathers, where there are feathers, lie flat and wrong, and it is heavier than the pale one, and it is colder in a different way. You have stood in front of the still water of the high tarn and looked at the two of them spread behind you and felt, every single time, that one of them belonged to you and one of them had been hung on you by mistake.  
You know which one your mother fears. She has never said it. She would never say it. But you have watched her face when the dark wing comes out, and you have seen the smallest flinch move through her before she smooths it away, and a child learns to read his mother's face before he learns anything else in the world. So you learned. You learned that one half of you brought that flinch into the person you loved most, and the other half did not, and you decided \- the way a boy decides things, completely and without telling anyone \- which half of yourself you were going to be.  
The disguise helps. You wear an amulet, an old thing she gave you, that lays an ordinary face over your own like still water over a stone. With it on you have chestnut hair and two plain eyes and a back with nothing folded into it, and you can walk down into Hearthdell and be no one in particular. Without it you have hair that goes from chestnut to a deep bruised violet underneath, and you have your mother's eye \- one of them, the right one, golden, warm, hers exactly \- and then you have the other eye, which is black from corner to corner with a red point of a pupil and a small ring of gold drowned somewhere inside it, and which you do not like to look at. You keep the amulet on. You keep it on the way other people keep their clothes on. It is not a disguise to you. It is just the face you wear in the world, and the other one is a thing that happens in the dark of your own house, in front of your own mother, and nowhere else.  
“You never show them,” she has told you, more times than you could count, in a voice that does not sound gentle the way the rest of her voice sounds gentle. “Not the wings. Not your face. Not the smallest part of it. Not for a friend, not for a kindness, not to save your own life. Do you understand me, Fane. There is nothing in this world worth being seen for.” And you would say yes, you understand, because you did understand, or you thought you did. You thought she was teaching you to be careful. It did not occur to you, not for years, that a mother who teaches her son so hard and so often to never be seen might be teaching him something she had learned somewhere, from someone, in a life she has never once described to you.  
❖  
You asked about your father exactly as often as any boy does, which is to say constantly, and then less, and then almost never, because you learned what happened to the question when you let it out of your mouth. Her face would close. Not angrily. That would have been easier. It closed the way a door closes in a house where everyone is being very polite, quietly, with a soft click, and behind it she would say the same few things she always said. That he had been a powerful man. That it had not worked between them. That she had left, and that leaving had been right, and that there was nothing on the other side of that door for you and never would be. And then she would change the subject so smoothly that you sometimes did not notice she had done it until you were already talking about something else.  
You have never even been sure where she came from. She is not from the valley \- that much is plain in her, in the pale almost-blue cast of her skin and the ice-light of her hair and the way she says certain words. But Hearthdell is full of people who came from somewhere, and you grew up assuming she was only one more of them, a woman who had walked a long way once and did not care to walk back through the telling of it. When you were a child you would sometimes try to make her tell you where, and she would only smile and say it did not matter, and that here was home, and that here was enough.  
There was one story she used to tell you, when you were small, that was yours and no one else's. She told it the way a person tells a child a thing to make the dark soft enough to sleep in \- in a low even voice with the light already out, the same words in the same order every time, so that you could say them along with her under your breath. It began, the way the best ones do, a long way off. Far from here, she would say, there was a land, and the land was dark. Not dark the way a room is dark when the candle is out. Dark all the way through, dark the way the bottom of a well is dark, a whole wide country with no light of its own and nothing in the sky to give it any.  
And in that dark land there were two brothers, and they had grown up side by side the way two trees grow up side by side, roots all tangled together under the ground so that no one could have said where the one ended and the other began. One of them was the quiet one, the watching one, pale and steady. And one day the other brother was hurt \- the story she told a small boy never said how, only that there had been a great wound, and that the hurt brother had broken, the way a branch breaks, into a hundred pieces. But the pieces did not fall, and they did not go out. They drifted. They scattered themselves up high across the dark, still glowing, faint and far apart, and the quiet brother went up after them \- because he would not leave his brother lying scattered in the cold \- and he hung there among the pieces and kept his watch over them, reaching and reaching and never quite closing his hand, and never once turning away. That was the part she always said slowest. He never turned away.  
And then, your mother said, when the watching brother had kept that watch for longer than there were words to measure, a grandmother came walking through the dark land below. She came from somewhere so far away the story had no name for it, alone, and tired, with the dust of the whole long road still on her \- and she looked up, and she saw the pale brother high above her grieving over his scattered one, and she did the thing that grandmothers do. She did not walk on. She climbed up after him, slow because she was old, and she settled herself behind the two of them, and she stayed. And that, your mother would say, is how the dark land got its sky. Look up, she would tell you, even though there was only the cottage roof above your bed \- look up, and there they are. The broken brother in all his hundred drifting pieces. The watching brother, the bright steady one, who followed him up and would not leave him. And the grandmother behind them both, the soft old light, dimmer than her grandchildren because she is old and full and round, keeping her watch so that no land has to be dark all the way through ever again. Three moons, where before there had been none. \- Then she would say that was enough now, and the dark would be soft, and you would sleep. You have not thought of that story in years. It is only a thing your mother used to tell you.  
❖  
You grew up, the way people do, into the village more than into the mountain. By twenty you knew every lane in Hearthdell and half the people in it knew you, and they liked you, which you will admit was a thing you worked at, because it was easy to be liked and being liked was a kind of safety. People who are charmed by you do not look hard at you. You had friends down in the valley, real ones, and you had Halloran.  
Halloran was old when you met him and older now, a man who had done a great many quiet things in a long life and settled at the end of it into the small green one of growing things and knowing things. He kept a garden that put your mother's to shame and he had, folded away in him, a little genuine magic \- cantrips, the small reliable spells, the kind of craft a person picks up across decades and never makes a fuss about. He saw something in you. You have wondered since whether he saw more than he ever said. But what he offered you was simple and it was the best thing anyone outside your own house had ever offered you: he offered to teach you.  
And here is the thing you have never been able to tell him, or your mother, or anyone. There is something wrong with your magic. Halloran would set you some small thing \- a light to be called up onto your palm, a pebble to be lifted, a working held steady across a count of ten \- and he would show you how, patiently, the shape of it, the way you reach and the way you hold, and you would do exactly as he showed you. And nothing would happen. Or worse than nothing. The magic your mother does, the warm kind, the bright kind, the kind that closes a wound and steadies a heartbeat, the kind that should come from the part of you with her golden eye and the pale frost-blue wing \- that magic does not answer you. You reach for it the way Halloran teaches and it is like reaching for something at the bottom of cold deep water, your fingers closing on nothing, again and again, while a kind old man waits and tells you to try once more. You have never once done it. Not the smallest spark of it. You have stood in his garden and failed the same simple working a hundred times and watched him hide his disappointment behind a gentleness that was somehow worse than the disappointment would have been.  
But there is the other side. And the other side answers. Up the mountain, in the dark, alone, with no one in the world to see it, you have tried Halloran's little tasks the only way that works for you \- by stopping. By not reaching the way he taught, by simply letting go of the refusal you carry all day long, and letting the thing that lives in your other half come up on its own. And it comes. It floods up out of you fast and certain and effortless, and it does the task. But it does it wrong. The light you call is not the soft warm light Halloran's hands make \- it comes up guttering and too dark at its heart, throwing shadows the wrong way. The pebble lifts, but it turns as it rises, slow and deliberate, as though something has taken hold of it. Every working you can actually complete comes out looking like a true spell that has been left out in bad weather \- the right shape, more or less, and something gone rotten in it. You have stood over your own results in the dark and felt no triumph at all, only a low cold dismay, because you did the thing, yes, but you saw what doing it made. So you have never shown a soul. Not Halloran \- you let him keep believing you were only a slow and earnest student, hopeless but trying. Not your mother. The moment you use that side for true, the amulet's quiet face comes apart; the disguise cannot hold over it, and your real self surfaces straight up through the illusion, the violet in your hair, the black eye, the wing. And so you simply did not do it where it could be seen. You have lived your whole life in that gap \- failing in the light where people could watch you fail, and only ever succeeding, distortedly, shamefully, in the dark. You did not know any other way to live.  
❖  
And then there was Lios.  
He came to Hearthdell that year with a small group of others, the way people sometimes did, travellers who meant to pass through and then found a reason to stay a while. He was your age. He was easy in a way you envied immediately and were drawn to just as fast, quick to laugh, quick to find you in a crowd, and from nearly the first day there was a thing between you that you did not have to work at the way you worked at everything else. You did not have to be charming for Lios. You only had to be there. You spent that summer in his company every hour you could find, and the valley, which you had always loved a little from a careful distance, became for a while a place you were simply, uncomplicatedly happy in.  
It moved toward something. You both knew it was moving toward something, in the way the light lengthens toward evening, and you found \- this surprised you \- that you wanted it. You wanted it enough to be afraid of it. Because you had spent your entire life behind one face, and there was rising in you, slow and terrifying and sweet, the wish to let one single person on the earth see the one behind it. Not the village. Not a friend, not a kindness, not to save your own life \- she had listed all of those, and she had been right about all of those. But this was none of those. This was Lios. And you thought, you let yourself think, that perhaps your mother's rule had simply never imagined a person you would want to be known by this much.  
So you showed him. One evening, alone with him, with your heart going like a hunted thing, you reached up and took off the amulet, and you let the still water fall away from the stone, and you let him see you. The violet, the golden eye, the black one. The wings, both of them, the pale and the dark, opened in the dusk behind you. You stood in front of the only person you had ever chosen to be seen by, and you waited.  
He did not say very much. That is the part you keep returning to, afterward, turning it over and over and finding no bottom to it. You had braced for fear, or for wonder, and you got neither. What moved across his face in the moment he saw you was something quick and quiet and almost like recognition, and then it was gone behind a smile that did not reach as far into him as his smiles had used to reach. He was kind. He said kind things. And he left earlier than he needed to, and there was a wrongness in the set of his shoulders as he went, and you stood there alone with the dusk coming down and you did not understand. You had shown him the truest thing you had. You had expected it to be the beginning of something. You could not have said, that night, what it had been the beginning of instead.  
You walked home through the dark with your chest hollowed out, going over it and over it, and you were so far down inside your own misery that you did not once think of the amulet. It was still in your hand, or in your pocket, somewhere \- not on you. You climbed the secluded path and pushed open the door of the house wearing your own face. The violet, the two mismatched eyes. You had not worn it across that threshold in living memory, and you were not thinking, and your mother was sitting up waiting for you the way she always waited for you, and she looked up, and she saw you.  
She came out of the chair so fast it went over. You had never seen her move like that, never in your life, and you had never seen her face do what it did \- the blood going out of it, her hands coming up. She was across the room and at the window with the shutter before you understood she was making sure no one could see in, and then she had you by the arms and she was asking you, in a voice with no gentleness left anywhere in it, what had happened, who had seen you, what you had done. And you were tired, and you were heartbroken, and it came out of you. You told her there was a boy. You told her his name. You told her you had shown him \- taken the amulet off and let him see all of you, the eyes, the wings, everything \- because you had wanted, just once, for someone to know you.  
You had thought, somewhere underneath, that she might understand that. She did not. What came up in her was not the careful fear you had lived alongside your whole life; it was something rawer and more frightened and it came out as anger. She told you that you should never have done it. That she had asked you for one thing, one, your entire life, and you had handed it away to a stranger because he had a nice face and a nice laugh. And something in you, already raw, already grieving, rose up to meet her \- because you would not have it, you would not let Lios be a stranger, not him. You told her she did not know him. You told her you did. You told her you were twenty years old and not a child she could keep folded up on a mountain forever, that you had a friend, a real one, the first thing in your life that had been only yours, and that you had known what you were doing.  
And the truth \- the truth you keep now in the cold honest place where you keep the things you will not lie to yourself about \- is that you did not know it. Even as you were shouting it at her, some small clear part of you was standing off to one side, remembering the wrongness in the set of Lios's shoulders, the smile that had not reached, the way he had left early. Some part of you had already seen that something had gone wrong. And you looked straight at that part of yourself, and you turned away from it, and you defended him anyway, because to admit she was right was to admit you had broken her one rule and ruined the best thing the year had given you and gotten nothing for it, and you could not, that night, carry that. So you chose him over what you had seen with your own eyes.  
And then you said the thing. You will hear yourself say it for the rest of your life. The argument had gone loud and gone ugly and you were past the edge of yourself, and you wanted, for one moment, to hurt her the exact amount she had hurt you, and so you reached for the cruelest true-sounding thing within reach and you threw it. You told her you wished you had never lived here. Any of it. The mountain, the hiding, the amulet, the secret, this small grey shut-away nothing of a life she had built for the two of you \- you said you wished you had never had to live it, that you wished you had been anyone else, born anywhere else, to anyone else. You watched it land. You watched what it did to her face, and even in the white heat of it some part of you knew you had just broken something, and you did not stop, you did not take it back. You did the thing a boy does. You turned around and you walked out of the house and into the dark, and you let the door slam on the both of you, and you did not look back to see her face.  
❖  
You went to your spot. You had had it since you were small \- a shoulder of bare rock a little way along the mountain, above the treeline, where the whole valley opened out below and the sky opened out above, and where you had gone your whole life to be no one's son and no one's secret for an hour. You went there now because you had nowhere else and because you could not stand to be in that house, and you sat down on the cold stone with the anger still going through you and the heartbreak underneath it, and you stared up. The night was clear. The stars were out, thick and uncountable, the way they only ever got over the high mountain, and you lay back on the rock and you looked at them until looking at them was the only thing left in you. And at some point, worn all the way through, you slept. You slept outdoors, under the open sky, with the cold of the stone coming up into your back, and the last thing you had ever said to your mother was that you wished you had never lived the life she gave you.  
❖  
You woke in the grey of early morning, stiff and cold and damp with the dew, with the valley below you still half-drowned in mist and the anger of the night before turned overnight into something heavier and worse. You wanted to take it back. Not all of it \- you still could not make yourself be sorry about Lios, not yet \- but you wanted to take back the shouting, the way you had left, the door. You wanted to go home and find your mother at the hearth and have it be a thing you could mend. So you got up off the cold stone and you started down the path, rehearsing it, the way you would say it, the way she would be angry and then not angry, and you came up the last secluded stretch toward the house in the thin morning light.  
You knew before you reached the door. There are things a person knows from the wrong shape of a familiar place, and the house was the wrong shape. The door stood open. There was no smoke from the chimney, and there should have been; your mother always had a fire by now. You ran the last of the path.  
Inside, everything that could be broken had been broken. The table was over. Her shelves of remedies were down, the little clay pots smashed across the floor, the dried herbs trodden into it. And there was blood. There was so much blood, dark across the boards in the dark of the house, more blood than you had a name for, and you stood in the doorway of your own home and could not make the room mean anything.  
Halloran was on the floor. He was alive, still, barely, and the blood was his and not all of it was his, and you went down onto your knees in it and got his head up and he was trying to speak to you. He lived along the mountain himself, his own path, his own secluded quiet, and he had been your mother's friend for as long as you had been alive; he came by the way he always came by, and this time he had come by while they were here. He had been in the house when they came, and he had tried. You understood that much from the state of him before he said a word. An old man with a garden and a handful of cantrips had stood between your mother and whatever had done this, and it had cost him everything, and it had not been enough.  
“Your mother,” he said. He kept losing the thread of it and finding it again. He told you he could not stop them. He told you there had been too many of them and they had been too fast and he was sorry, he was so sorry, and you were saying something back to him, you do not remember what, you were telling him to be quiet and save himself and it was already far too late for that. And then he gave you the only thing he had left to give you. He had heard them. Before the dark took the edges of the room he had heard them say where they were taking her, and he said the name of it to you, a place you knew, a place every soul in the valley knew \- the old boundary cairn at the head of the path where the homestead land gives out and the Serratus treeline begins. He told you that was where they had gone. He made you say it back to him. And then Halloran, who had offered you the kindest thing anyone outside your own blood ever had, drew one more breath, and did not draw the one after it.  
❖  
You do not have a clear memory of the ground between the house and the cairn. You know you ran. You know the night went past you in pieces, the pines, the cold, the path you could have walked blind, and that you ran the way an animal runs, with nothing in you but the running. You came up out of the trees to the old cairn at the edge of the Serratus wild fully expecting to find them \- soldiers, your mother, something to fight, something to beg, something. You found nothing. The cairn stood there the way it had stood your whole life. The dark forest breathed beyond it. There was no one.  
You almost broke, then. You almost simply went down on the cold ground and stopped. But you made yourself look \- because there had to be something, there had to be, the alternative was unthinkable \- and looking, you found it. Not on the ground. In the air. A little way past the cairn the night itself was wrong, was split, a long ragged seam hanging in nothing where the dark of the world had been pulled open like cloth. And through the gap in it there was somewhere else.  
You went to it. You could not have made yourself not go to it. Through the tear the air was colder and stranger and it was not the valley's night on the other side \- it was a deeper dark, a wide still dark, and hung up in it, over a low and shadowed land, were three moons. Three of them, together. And the story came up through you all at once, the old soft story told to a small boy to make the dark sleepable \- the watching brother, the broken one drifting in his pieces, the tired grandmother who came from far away and stayed \- and you stood looking at the three of them burning quietly over a land that should not exist, and you understood with a lurch that went all the way down to the floor of you that it had not been a story. It had been a map. It had been the only true thing she had ever told you about where she came from, and she had folded it small and put it inside a thing for children so that you would carry it your whole life and never once know you were carrying it.  
There was blood on the pale growth near the seam. A little, dark, fresh. You could not know it was hers. You will be honest with yourself, here, in the place where you keep the things you actually know \- you could not know. But you were past the country where a person waits to be certain. You stepped up to the torn place in the world, and you did not let yourself think, and you went through.  
❖  
The cold met you first, and the dark, and the wrongness of standing somewhere that the soles of your feet did not believe in. You straightened up out of a crouch you did not remember dropping into, and you looked around you at where the tear had let you out, and you tried to make this new place mean something.  
Above you, through the gaps in a black tangle of branches, there were three moons. You saw them and the bedtime story rose in you all at once, the watching brother and the broken one drifting in his hundred pieces and the tired grandmother who came from far away and stayed, and you understood with a lurch that went all the way down to the floor of you that it had not been a story. It had been the truth of where she came from, folded small and put inside a thing for children so that you would carry it your whole life and never once know you were carrying it. And around you, holding up those moons, there was a forest of the dead. Trees without leaves and without bark, bone-grey, clawed up at the sky, grown so thick in places that the moonlight could not reach the ground, and thorns where there should have been green, and a silence like the inside of a held breath. There was no path. There was no sound. There was you, and the dead trees, and the tear still hanging open behind you and bleeding its slow fire into the dark.  
And then there was sound. Voices, somewhere off through the trees, and the dull tread of feet, the creak and clink of people on the move, and all of it coming nearer. The instinct your mother had spent twenty years drilling into your body took you before your mind had finished deciding anything \- you went down and back, in among the roots and the bramble, and you made yourself small and still and silent, and you watched.  
They came to the tear. A whole company of them, more than you could quickly count, and you had no words for any of what you were looking at. There were armed men in plain steel and deep red who spread into a loose ring around the wound, their backs to it and their faces to the dark, watching the trees the way men watch for something to come out of them. There were others fully armoured in a black so deep it drank the firelight, with red showing at their throats and wrists beneath it, and over the lower halves of their faces they wore masks the pale colour of bone, their eyes left bare above. And three more in that same black-and-red came forward to the very edge of the seam and lifted their hands toward it.  
One of the bone-masked ones spoke as they took their places, and his voice carried to you on the still cold air, bored and put-upon.  
“Third one this cycle. I had hoped to be back before the dark-meal.”  
“The forest will not have shifted itself to suit your appetite. Stand it and seal it.”  
The second voice had not been loud. It had not needed to be. It came from the one who stood apart from all the others, out in front of the work, and it landed on the bone-masked one like a flat hand laid on the back of his neck, and he said nothing further. You looked at the one who had spoken, and the feeling that came off him went down your spine like cold water poured straight in at the collar. He wore the black and the underlying red like the rest, but finer, made for a man the others answered to, and he wore no helm. Over the lower half of his face there was a mask of pale bone, and above it his face was bare \- pale, paler than any face you had ever seen, pale the way old paper is pale, and the eyes set in it caught the firelight and gave it back wrong, a red buried somewhere far down inside them. He did not move. He only stood and let the work happen around him, and there was a wrongness to him that you could not have pointed at and could not look away from.  
The three at the seam began to speak together. Low, and even, and exactly matched, the same words from three mouths in the same instant, and where their joined voices touched the edge of the wound the fire of it dimmed and the lightning of it stilled and the gap began, slowly, to draw closed. And it was costing them. You could see it even from where you hid \- their voices held steady but their bodies did not, one of them swaying on his feet, another with his head bowed as though the work were a weight strapped across his shoulders, all three pouring something out of themselves into the air faster than a body could afford to lose it. You watched the seam draw down and down. You watched the slow fire gutter and shrink. You watched the last thin thread of the way home knit shut into plain unbroken dark \- and your mother was not here, you had searched the whole company for her the entire time, and there was no struggling woman, no pale face, no ice-light hair, nothing of her anywhere among them, and the hope that had carried you through the tear went out of you all at once and left you hollow and cold in the roots.  
The work finished. The three who had sealed it could barely stand; one went down to his knees and was hauled up by an arm, and they did not rest, they simply turned and went, slow and emptied and swaying, because the pale one in front had already turned to go and the whole company moved when he moved. You did not have it in you to fight them, and your mother was not theirs to take back. But they were the only living things in this whole dark country that you had laid eyes on, and they had come from somewhere and were going back to somewhere, and that was the only thread in your hand. So you did the thing she had made you good at without either of you ever knowing what she was making you good at for. You went unseen. You followed.  
The pale one led them through the dead forest, and he led them by a stone. You saw it in his hand when the firelight still reached him \- a small worked piece of green, jade or something near it, that he would lift and consider and then turn by, the way another man might read a road. And the forest needed reading. You came to understand that slowly, following at the edge of sight, because the forest would not hold still. Never while you watched it. You would fix your eyes on some particular knot of dead trees ahead and then look away to set your feet, and when you looked back the trees would be standing somewhere they had not been, or a way that had been open would be closed over with bramble, or the dark behind you would have a different shape than the dark you had walked out of. You never once caught it moving. It simply, between one glance and the next, had moved. You kept the green stone in your sight as much as you kept the men themselves, because you understood without being told that if you lost them, you lost the only thing in this place that knew the way.  
And then the dead forest broke, and the green of what lay past it hit you like a held breath let go. Below you and ahead of you a whole country opened out \- a wide bowl of living land, ringed all around by the dead and the thorned, green the way you had not seen green since your own valley. And out of the heart of it there rose a tree.  
You had no scale for it. That was the first thing, the thing your eye kept refusing \- it was a tree the size of a country. It went up out of the land and it kept going, a trunk broad as a whole town set on its end, and its canopy spread across the dark overhead like a second sky, and the canopy was not green. It was a deep glowing violet, lit from somewhere inside itself, and down through the trunk and out along the great limbs ran veins of a cold blue light, slow and bright, as though the tree had something luminous moving in it the way blood moves in a body. And it was not empty. Built up around its base, climbing into its lower reaches, there was a city \- pale walls and white towers crowding the foot of the tree and rising into it, spires lifting one behind another, all of it small as a toy against the vastness of the thing it grew on. A wide river caught the light and threw it back, winding out from the city through the green in pale shining branches, and pale roads ran alongside the water, and away across the bowl of the land you could see other places, smaller, scattered \- clusters of roof and lantern-light set far apart from one another, a glow of warm windows here, a stand of strange-coloured trees there, the whole country stitched together by the river and the roads. There was so much life in it. After the dead forest, after the unending dark, after a world that had shown you nothing but its ruin, you stood at the treeline and looked at an entire living realm gathered and shining in the middle of all that death, and there was nothing else a person could do with a sight that size but stand and look at it.  
The crew did not turn toward it. They kept to a road that ran along its edge, and you kept to them, hanging back in the last of the cover \- and you had not gone far along that road when scouts nearly had you. Lithe forest folk, elven-looking, quick, and a shout went up that was about you, and you ran. You ran the second desperate flight of a night that would not end, off the road and blind into the green, putting distance and then more distance between yourself and the voices, and you hid, and you waited, and you moved, and you hid again. Hours of it. Heart-in-the-throat hours, until at last the voices were gone and the green had closed over you, and you came, footsore and hollowed out and starving, back within sight of that same road \- and a little way along it, a strange small settlement of folk who seemed half made of growing things, bark-skinned and leaf-crowned, a quiet hamlet sitting astride the way like a waystation for travellers bound somewhere further on.  
You did not go to them. You could not make yourself trust a single living thing in this place. You took fruit from the edge of their groves like a thief, because your body had begun to fail you and you had not eaten since a life ago, and while you were looking for somewhere to be unseen and still you found a way down. An opening, at the hamlet's edge \- old stone, a dark mouth in the ground, ruins going under. And down was the one direction left that no one would follow you into, so you took it.  
❖  
It went deep. It went down through worked stone that had not been walked in a very long time, ruined and root-broken, the architecture of something dead, and then down further still into the true and ancient dark beneath all of it, a maze of tunnel and old temple and pale strange fungi giving off the only light there was. You went down until there was no possibility of being heard by anything that lived in the world above. And it was there, finally, alone, deeper than anyone could follow, that the thing you had been holding shut since the doorway of your own house came apart.  
You screamed. It was not a word. You had not slept since the cold morning you woke on your own mountain a whole impossible day and a world ago, your mother was gone through a door you had watched seal, Halloran's blood was dried into the knees of your clothes, the only person you had ever chosen to be seen by had looked at you and walked away \- and the last thing you ever said to your mother, the last words she will ever have of you, were that you wished you had never lived the life she had given up everything to give you. You had slammed a door on her. And you would never, ever be able to take it back. You stood in the bottom of a dead world and you let all of it out of you at once in a sound that had no shape. And the disguise could not survive it. With the rage came the truth of you, surfacing up through the amulet's quiet face the way it always did, and this time you let it \- the violet rising in your hair, the black eye opening, and your wings, both of them, breaking free across the dark of that ruined place.  
There was a still pool near where you stood, and the fungi laid their faint light across it, and you saw yourself in it. You saw the pale wing. And you saw the other one \- the dark one, the scaled one, the heavy wrong one, the one that had moved that flinch across your mother's face every day of your remembered life, the one half of yourself you had decided, at some age too young to remember deciding it, that you did not want and would not be. And you looked at it lying there in your reflection in the underground water, in the worst hour you had ever lived, with nothing and no one left to perform a self for, and you decided you were going to take it off.  
You will not soften this for yourself. You did it slowly because there was no way to do it fast. You did it with your own hands and what you had, and the dark wing did not want to come away from you, because it was you, it was rooted in you, it was meat and your meat, and you cut it from yourself anyway. The pain was enormous and you took it. The blood came and came, black-dark in the fungal light, and it ran off into the still pool and bloomed through it and turned the water red, and you did not stop. You bore down on it and you kept on, through the worst of it and past the worst of it, until the dark wing came finally and fully free of your back and you were holding it, severed, in the wreck of a temple at the bottom of a world that was not yours.

You left it there. You walked away from it down the tunnel and you left a trail behind you, a long unbroken line of your own blood across the old stone, marking every step of where you had been. You went until you found a turning deep enough and quiet enough and far enough from the wing, a hollow in the ruin where the fungus-light barely reached, and you folded down into the corner of it. One wing now. Pale, ragged, soaked dark and heavy with your own blood. And you sat in the corner of the dark with your back against cold worked stone older than anything you had a name for, in the deep of a place that felt as though no living thing had walked it in a hundred lifetimes, alone, further from home than the word home could reach, and you cried. And the tears, where they caught the faint light and ran clear, carried in them the smallest, faintest hint of gold.
